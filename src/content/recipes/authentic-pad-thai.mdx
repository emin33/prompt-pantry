## Overview

This recipe focuses on recreating the complex, balanced flavors of street-style Pad Thai. Key techniques include cold-soaking rice noodles to prevent mushiness, caramelizing palm sugar for a deeply nuanced sauce, and high-heat stir-frying in small batches to achieve the coveted 'wok hei' – a smoky, charred essence that elevates the dish.

## Steps

### 1. Prepare Noodles & Mise en Place

Place the thin flat rice noodles in a large bowl. Cover them completely with room temperature water and soak for 20-30 minutes, or until pliable but still firm and slightly resistant to the bite. They should be chewable but unpleasant to eat raw, as they will finish cooking in the wok. Drain well and set aside. While noodles soak, prepare all remaining ingredients (slice shallots, mince garlic, chop chives, cube tofu, peel shrimp, crush peanuts, etc.) and arrange them around your cooking station. This 'mise en place' is crucial for fast stir-frying.

### 2. Make the Pad Thai Sauce

In a small stainless steel skillet or saucepan, combine the finely chopped palm sugar and 0.5 cup water. Cook over medium heat, stirring occasionally, until the palm sugar completely melts and begins to lightly caramelize, turning a slightly darker brown. This caramelization adds depth to the sauce. Immediately add the tamarind paste and fish sauce, stirring well until everything is combined and the sugar is fully dissolved. The sauce should be dark, slightly thick, and intensely savory, sour, and sweet. Taste and adjust if necessary, keeping in mind it will mellow when mixed with noodles. Set aside.

### 3. Sear Tofu (Batch 1)

Heat 1 tablespoon of peanut oil in your wok over high heat until it just begins to smoke. Add half of the extra-firm tofu cubes and spread them in a single layer. Cook, undisturbed, for 2-3 minutes until golden brown and crispy on one side. Flip and cook for another 1-2 minutes until browned on a second side. Remove the tofu from the wok and set aside. Repeat with 1 more tablespoon of peanut oil and the remaining tofu, then remove and set aside with the first batch.

### 4. Stir-Fry First Batch (2 Servings)

Add 1 tablespoon of peanut oil to the wok and return to high heat until smoking. Add half of the thinly sliced shallots and half of the minced garlic, stirring rapidly for 15-30 seconds until fragrant. Add half of the chopped dried shrimp (if using) and half of the finely chopped preserved salted radish, stir-frying for another 30 seconds. Push the aromatics to one side of the wok. Add half of the shrimp to the empty side and stir-fry until just turning pink, about 1-2 minutes. Add half of the drained rice noodles to the wok, tossing vigorously to combine with the aromatics and shrimp. Pour in half of the prepared Pad Thai sauce, tossing continuously for about 1 minute until the noodles are evenly coated and the sauce is absorbed. The noodles should become translucent and chewy.

### 5. Incorporate Egg & Vegetables (Batch 1)

Push the noodle mixture to one side of the wok, creating an empty space. Add 0.5 tablespoon of peanut oil to this space, then pour in half of the lightly beaten eggs. Let the egg set for a few seconds, then gently scramble it with your wok spatula, breaking it into rough pieces. Once mostly set but still slightly moist, incorporate the egg into the noodles and other ingredients. Add half of the seared tofu, half of the bean sprouts, and half of the chopped garlic chives. Toss everything together for about 20-30 seconds, just until the bean sprouts are slightly softened but still crisp, and the chives are wilted. Immediately transfer the finished Pad Thai to a serving plate.

### 6. Stir-Fry Second Batch (2 Servings)

Repeat steps 4 and 5 exactly for the remaining half of the ingredients (shallots, garlic, dried shrimp, radish, shrimp, noodles, sauce, eggs, tofu, bean sprouts, garlic chives). It's crucial to cook in two separate batches to maintain high heat and achieve the proper texture, preventing the noodles from steaming and becoming soggy. Transfer the second batch to the serving plate or plates.

### 7. Garnish and Serve

Garnish the Pad Thai generously with crushed roasted peanuts, fresh lime wedges, and extra chopped garlic chives. Offer red chili flakes or powder and raw cane sugar on the side for diners to customize the balance of flavors and heat to their preference. Serve immediately while hot.

## Notes

- **Noodle Perfection**: Do not over-soak or boil your rice noodles; they should still be firm after soaking as they will finish cooking in the wok. Overcooked noodles will be mushy and break apart easily. If your noodles feel too soft after soaking, reduce the soaking time next time.
- **Sauce Balance is Key**: The Pad Thai sauce should be intensely flavored on its own. If it tastes too sweet, add a touch more fish sauce or tamarind. If it's too sour or salty, add a pinch more palm sugar. Achieving this balance before adding to the noodles is vital for a truly authentic taste.
- **Wok Hei at Home**: To get a smoky 'wok hei' flavor on a home stove, always preheat your wok until it's smoking hot before adding oil, and cook in small batches (no more than 2 servings at a time). This prevents the temperature from dropping too low, which would steam the ingredients instead of stir-frying them.
- **Ingredient Substitutions**: If you can't find palm sugar, light brown sugar is an acceptable substitute, though it lacks the nuanced caramel flavor. For tamarind, a good quality jarred tamarind concentrate (Thai origin preferred) can be used in place of pulp, but taste and adjust for sourness as concentrations vary. If omitting dried shrimp, add a tiny pinch of salt to the sauce for extra savory depth.
- **Storage**: Pad Thai is best enjoyed fresh. Leftovers can be stored in an airtight container in the refrigerator for up to 2 days. Reheat gently in a wok or skillet over medium heat, adding a splash of water if the noodles seem dry. The noodles may lose some of their springiness upon reheating.
