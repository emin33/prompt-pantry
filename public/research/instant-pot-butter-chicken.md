# The definitive guide to Instant Pot butter chicken

**The best Instant Pot butter chicken recipes span a spectrum from a 30-minute dump-and-go method to near-restaurant-authentic approaches that preserve the tandoori char and whole-spice depth of traditional murgh makhani.** After surveying the most acclaimed recipes from Indian food bloggers, professional test kitchens, and top-rated community sources, one truth emerges: the core technique that separates good from great is what happens *after* pressure cooking — blending the sauce smooth, stirring in butter and cream off-heat, and finishing with kasuri methi and a fresh hit of garam masala. The recipes below range from Urvashi Pitre's viral no-sauté method to Serious Eats' rigorous two-component adaptation, giving home cooks options at every skill and time level.

Butter chicken (murgh makhani) was invented in 1950s Delhi at Moti Mahal restaurant to repurpose leftover tandoori chicken in a tomato-butter-cream sauce. The Instant Pot cannot replicate a 900°F clay tandoor, but the best adaptations find clever workarounds — broiling yogurt-coated chicken, cooking it on a trivet above the sauce, or simply accepting that pressure-infused flavor is a worthy trade-off for a weeknight dinner.

## Eight recipes ranked from simplest to most authentic

### 1. TwoSleevers (Urvashi Pitre) — the recipe that started it all

Urvashi Pitre, an Indian-American Ph.D. and author of six cookbooks, posted this recipe in March 2017 to a 1.8-million-member Facebook group. It went viral, earned her the nickname "The Butter Chicken Lady," and spawned a cookbook deal. With **1,890+ comments** and coverage in The New Yorker and the New York Times, it remains the single most referenced Instant Pot butter chicken recipe online.

**The method is radically simple**: one 14-oz can of diced tomatoes, garlic, ginger, and all ground spices go directly into the pot. Chicken thighs sit on top. No sautéing, no browning, no onions — Pitre is emphatic: "no onions for the love of all that's good." After **10 minutes on high pressure with 10 minutes natural release**, she removes the chicken, blends the sauce smooth with an immersion blender, then stirs in **4 oz cubed butter, 4 oz heavy cream, and a second teaspoon of garam masala**. The spice blend is turmeric, cayenne, smoked paprika, garam masala, and cumin. No kasuri methi, no whole spices.

What makes it work is the intentional simplicity: the pressure cooker extracts deep flavor from canned tomatoes and garlic, and the post-cook butter emulsification creates surprising richness. Pitre deliberately makes extra sauce for repurposing — her "Now & Later" concept suggests butter chicken pizza on naan with the leftovers. The recipe is also keto-adaptable (coconut oil and coconut milk for dairy-free).

### 2. Piping Pot Curry (Meeta Arora) — whole spices in a pouch, everything blended

This clever method eliminates sautéing while retaining the aromatic depth of whole spices. Arora places **cinnamon, cardamom, cloves, and bay leaf in a cheesecloth pouch**, then dumps it into the pot alongside roughly chopped onions, tomatoes, optional cashews, chicken thighs, Kashmiri chili powder, and turmeric. After **8 minutes on high pressure with 10 minutes natural release**, she discards the spice pouch, removes the chicken, and blends everything else into a silky sauce. The finishing stir adds cream, honey, garam masala, and kasuri methi.

The honey is a distinctive touch that balances tomato acidity beautifully, and the cashew option adds natural body without flour or cornstarch. Rated **4.90 out of 5**, this recipe bridges the dump-and-go convenience of Pitre's approach with the aromatic complexity that whole spices provide. It also adapts seamlessly to paneer butter masala or butter tofu.

### 3. Ministry of Curry (Archana Mundhe) — the healthier coconut milk version

Archana Mundhe, author of *The Essential Indian Instant Pot Cookbook* (authorized by the Instant Pot brand), developed a paleo-friendly version that replaces butter and heavy cream with **full-fat coconut milk and ghee**. Her key technique: refrigerate the coconut milk can overnight, then separate the thick cream from the watery liquid. The watery portion goes into the pot for cooking; the rich cream is stirred in at the end.

She sautés onions in ghee, blooms ginger-garlic with turmeric and **Kashmiri red chili powder** (for color without heat), then pressure cooks chicken in fresh tomato purée for **8 minutes on high with quick release**. The finishing step layers in coconut cream, tomato paste, garam masala, **2 tablespoons of kasuri methi**, and sugar. That generous kasuri methi addition — double what most recipes use — is what pushes this toward authentic restaurant flavor despite the dairy-free base. A final 5-minute simmer on sauté mode thickens the sauce.

### 4. Spice Cravings (Aneesha Gupta) — yogurt marinade with trivet cooking

This is the most traditionally structured Instant Pot adaptation. Gupta, author of *The Electric Pressure Cooker Curry Cookbook*, insists on **marinating chicken in yogurt, garam masala, ginger-garlic paste, Kashmiri chili, lemon juice, and salt** for at least 30 minutes (overnight preferred). Her ingenious Instant Pot trick: place the marinated chicken **on a trivet above the sauce** during pressure cooking, so the steam cooks the chicken while the marinade flavors seal in — a clever substitute for tandoor grilling.

For larger batches or special occasions, she offers a second option: **broil the marinated chicken in the oven at high heat for 8 minutes per side** to develop authentic char, then add it to the finished sauce. This two-track approach lets weeknight cooks use the trivet shortcut while entertainers can get closer to restaurant results. The sauce itself is straightforward: butter, ginger-garlic paste, puréed tomatoes, and spices, finished with cream and brown sugar.

### 5. Pressure Cook Recipes (Amy + Jacky) — the browning-and-umami maximalist

With a **4.91 rating from 164 votes and 32,000+ shares**, this is the most elaborate popular recipe — and arguably the closest to restaurant depth among the blog versions. Amy and Jacky take three steps most recipes skip: they **brown chicken thighs in ghee** (3 minutes per side on sauté), they sauté onions and spices in the rendered fat, and they add an unexpected ingredient — **3 tablespoons of soy sauce** as an umami booster.

The tomato component is **1 cup of tomato paste layered on top without stirring** (a critical burn-prevention technique). Cook time is a brief **6 minutes on high pressure with 10 minutes natural release**. The finishing sauce is yogurt-heavy: **1½ cups plain yogurt plus ⅓ cup heavy cream**, stirred in after cooking with lemon juice for brightness. The Maillard flavors from browning, the umami depth from soy sauce, and the tangy yogurt-cream balance make this the most complex flavor profile among the popular recipes. Reviewers consistently call it "the closest to restaurant-quality."

### 6. Milk Street (Christopher Kimball) — cashew cream replaces all dairy

Kimball's test kitchen takes a nutritionally distinctive path: **roasted salted cashews puréed with water** replace heavy cream entirely, and the recipe uses just **3 tablespoons of butter** total. Both the cashews and the canned tomatoes are pre-blended before cooking — double blender work, but it produces an ultra-smooth sauce with a subtly nutty richness.

After sautéing onion, garlic, ginger, and spices in butter, the chicken and tomato purée cook for **10 minutes on high pressure with quick release** (unusual — most recipes use natural release). The cashew cream and remaining butter are stirred in during a 4–5 minute low-sauté simmer. The result is lighter than cream-based versions but surprisingly rich, with a distinct nuttiness. This method has roots in traditional Indian cooking, where cashew paste (or melon seed paste) has always been used as a sauce thickener.

### 7. America's Test Kitchen — tomato paste innovation with broiled chicken

ATK's rigorously tested version makes two distinctive choices. First, they use **½ cup tomato paste dissolved in 1½ cups water** instead of canned or fresh tomatoes — delivering concentrated flavor, bright acidity, and deep color without excess liquid. Second, they **broil yogurt-coated chicken thighs** on a wire rack until charred, leveraging a scientific insight: yogurt's milk proteins and lactose brown quickly under a broiler, mimicking tandoor char.

The sauce builds on a sautéed onion-garlic-ginger base with a full tablespoon of garam masala, coriander, and cumin, finished with **1 cup heavy cream** and butter whisked in at the end. The broiled chicken is cut into pieces and stirred into the warm sauce. While not strictly an Instant Pot recipe (the sauce is stovetop), the technique principles — especially the tomato paste method and broiler char — translate directly to pressure cooker adaptations and represent ATK's answer to the question of how to get restaurant-quality results at home.

### 8. Serious Eats (Sho Spaeth) — the most authentic pressure cooker version

This is the gold standard for home cooks willing to invest about 75 minutes for near-restaurant authenticity. The key insight: "the texture of pressure-cooked chicken was wanting," so **the chicken broils while the sauce pressure-cooks simultaneously**, adding zero extra time.

The chicken gets a proper **yogurt marinade with garam masala, black salt (kala namak), grated ginger, and ground fenugreek leaves**, then broils for ~14 minutes until charred. Meanwhile, the sauce starts with **toasted whole fenugreek, chiles de arbol, brown cardamom, and clove** ground with garam masala. Cashews softened in tomato liquid are blended into the sauce for body. After pressure cooking, the sauce is immersion-blended and finished with butter and cream. The 21-ingredient list and parallel cooking technique make this the most technically demanding recipe surveyed — but also the one that preserves every traditional element: tandoori char, whole toasted spices, cashew thickening, kasuri methi, and black salt.

## What separates good from restaurant-quality

The research revealed a clear hierarchy of techniques that incrementally improve results. Understanding these allows any cook to upgrade whichever base recipe they choose.

**Kasuri methi is the single most important finishing ingredient.** Every Indian cooking expert surveyed identified dried fenugreek leaves as the defining flavor of authentic butter chicken. The technique is specific: crush the dried leaves between your palms to release aromatic oils, then stir into the finished sauce. Use **½ to 1 tablespoon** per recipe serving 4–6. Multiple sources describe it as "the difference between home cooking and restaurant quality." There is no substitute — if you cannot find it, omit it rather than replacing it. Let the finished dish rest 10–15 minutes after adding kasuri methi so the flavor permeates the sauce.

**Butter and cream must go in after pressure cooking.** This is the most universally agreed-upon rule. Pitre explains: "pressure cooking causes butter and heavy cream to separate into a greasy mess. Adding it afterward allows the fat to emulsify properly." Let the sauce cool for 3–5 minutes before stirring in dairy. One advanced technique from Swasthi's Recipes: temper the cream by mixing a few tablespoons of hot sauce into the cream in a separate bowl first, then pour the warmed cream mixture back into the pot. Never re-pressurize sauce containing cream.

**A second addition of garam masala at finishing transforms aroma.** Several top recipes add garam masala in two stages — once before cooking for depth, once after for fragrance. The volatile aromatic compounds in garam masala dissipate under pressure, so that final teaspoon stirred in with the cream restores the warm, complex scent. Homemade garam masala makes a noticeable difference over store-bought; at minimum, ensure your blend is fresh and not stale.

**Sauce blending and optional straining create the signature texture.** Restaurant butter chicken is velvety smooth — never chunky. After pressure cooking, remove the chicken, then blend the sauce with an immersion blender (or cool slightly and use a regular blender). For the next level, strain through a fine-mesh sieve, pressing with a spatula. This removes fibrous bits from tomato skins, onion, or cardamom pods. Whisking cold cubed butter into the hot-but-not-boiling sauce creates a glossy, emulsified finish.

## The five most common mistakes and how to avoid them

**The burn notice** is the number-one Instant Pot complaint across all recipes. It occurs when thick tomato products caramelize on the heating element. Three solutions work reliably: layer tomato sauce or paste on top without stirring, deglaze thoroughly after sautéing by scraping all browned bits, and ensure at least **½ cup of thin liquid** (broth, water, or tomato juice) touches the bottom of the pot. Newer Instant Pot models (post-2020) have more sensitive burn detection — add an extra ¼ cup of cold water if using these.

**Watery, thin sauce** results from adding too much liquid. Pitre's advice is direct: "Any more water and it's going to taste insipid." Chicken and tomatoes release substantial liquid under pressure. If the sauce is too thin after cooking, simmer on sauté mode to reduce before adding cream (which will thin it further). Cashew paste or blending adds body without extra liquid.

**Bland flavor** almost always traces to insufficient garam masala, stale spices, or missing kasuri methi. Use fresh garlic and ginger rather than powdered. Taste aggressively and adjust salt at the end — under-salting is the most common seasoning error. A teaspoon of sugar or honey should be added to balance tomato acidity; this is standard in traditional recipes, not a shortcut.

**Overcooked chicken** happens with excessive pressure time. Boneless thighs need **6–10 minutes** at high pressure; boneless breasts need only **5 minutes**. Quick release prevents carryover cooking. Placing chicken on top of the sauce (not buried) and removing it before blending also protects texture.

**Greasy or broken sauce** comes from adding butter and cream before pressure cooking, or from boiling the sauce aggressively after adding dairy. The emulsification must happen gently, off-heat or on low sauté, with the butter cut into cold cubes and stirred in gradually.

## Choosing the right recipe for your situation

The spectrum of approaches maps neatly to different cooking contexts. For a **weeknight dinner in 30 minutes**, Pitre's dump-and-go method delivers remarkable flavor for zero active effort. For **deeper flavor with moderate effort**, the Piping Pot Curry whole-spice-pouch method or Amy + Jacky's browning approach each add 15 minutes of active work for significantly more complexity. The Ministry of Curry recipe is the best **dairy-free or paleo option**, and Milk Street's cashew cream version is the strongest **lighter alternative**.

For **impressing guests**, the Serious Eats parallel-cooking method or the Spice Cravings broiler option come closest to authentic restaurant results. The traditional elements that matter most, in descending order of impact, are: kasuri methi in the finishing, the char on yogurt-marinated chicken, whole toasted spices in the sauce base, cashew paste for body, and sauce straining for texture. Each element you add moves the needle toward authenticity.

One insight from Tea for Turmeric's 20-time testing deserves the final word: "The recipe became incrementally better the more I pared it down, eventually getting closer to the original Moti Mahal recipe." The inventor of butter chicken was a Peshawar immigrant who cooked simply, with fewer spices than most modern recipes use. Sometimes the best butter chicken is the one that trusts its core ingredients — good tomatoes, real butter, fresh cream, and kasuri methi — to do the work.
