# The complete science of exceptional pizza

**The single biggest insight across all expert sources is unanimous: the fastest path to transcendent pizza is not a faster recipe — it's a 48–72 hour cold ferment using almost no yeast, baked on a preheated steel under a broiler.** This approach requires only 10 minutes of hands-on work, costs pennies in ingredients, and produces dough with the complex flavor, perfect extensibility, and Maillard browning that separates cult-following pizza from forgettable pizza. Every acclaimed pizzaiolo — from Tony Gemignani's 13 competition titles to Dan Richer's James Beard award to Kenji López-Alt's food-science rigor — converges on some version of this principle: less yeast, more time, maximum heat. The research below documents exactly how and where they agree, where they diverge, and what the food-truck operational lens reveals about producing this quality at speed.

## The authorities agree on more than they disagree

Eight of the most respected pizza sources were analyzed in depth: Kenji López-Alt (Serious Eats/The Food Lab), Tony Gemignani (13-time World Pizza Champion), Chris Bianco (Pizzeria Bianco, first pizzaiolo to win a James Beard Award), Jeff Varasano (home pizza obsessive turned restaurateur), Roberta's Pizza (Brooklyn), Ken Forkish (Flour Water Salt Yeast / The Elements of Pizza), Dan Richer (Razza, Jersey City — named "Best Pizza in NY" by the New York Times despite being in New Jersey), and Peter Reinhart (American Pie, The Bread Baker's Apprentice).

**Where all sources converge:**

Every single authority insists on weighing ingredients by gram, not measuring by volume. Every one endorses cold fermentation as producing superior flavor. Every one demands the oven be as hot as possible with a 45–60 minute preheat on stone or steel. Every one says less is more on toppings. Every one says quality flour matters most among ingredients. Every one agrees baking steel outperforms stone in a home oven. Every one says minimal handling preserves gas structure — gentle shaping, never a rolling pin for artisan styles.

**Where they diverge sharply — and what it means:**

The deepest fault line is **oil and sugar in the dough**. Bianco, Varasano, Forkish, and Richer use zero — purist lean doughs of flour, water, salt, and yeast only. Kenji, Gemignani, and Reinhart add both oil and sugar (or malt), arguing these are essential for browning in a 500°F home oven where Maillard reactions need help. Roberta's splits the difference with minimal oil (1.3% of flour weight). For a home oven capped at 550°F, the evidence favors including a small amount of sugar or diastatic malt powder and a modest amount of olive oil — these compensate for the temperature gap without compromising character.

**Hydration** is another divide. Forkish advocates **70%** for home ovens; Gemignani and traditional NY land at **58–65%**; Dan Richer goes as high as **76% for home ovens** and 68% for pizza ovens. The counterintuitive insight here: higher hydration produces a *crispier* crust in a home oven, because the longer bake time evaporates more moisture — so you need more water to start with to avoid a dry, tough result. The sweet spot for a home oven on steel is **65–70%**.

**Sauce philosophy** splits cleanly by style. Neapolitan purists (Varasano, Bianco, Mangieri of Una Pizza Napoletana) insist on raw crushed tomatoes — never cooked. Kenji's NY-style recipe simmers sauce for an hour with butter, garlic, and herbs. For a neo-Neapolitan food-truck-minded approach, raw sauce is actually the faster *and* better option: hand-crushed quality tomatoes with salt take under 5 minutes to prepare, preserve bright tomato flavor, and cook sufficiently on the pizza itself even in a home oven.

**Salt percentage** is an outlier worth noting: Varasano uses **3.5%** (unusually high), while most others cluster around **2–2.5%**. Reinhart identifies 2.5% as the sweet spot.

## Dough: hydration, flour, fermentation, and the fastest path to great

### Hydration ranges and what each feels like

At **60%**, dough is predictable and easy to handle — dense crumb, fewer air pockets, classic Neapolitan texture. At **65%**, the sweet spot for home ovens emerges: soft, workable, stretches cleanly, delivers a crispy bottom with chewy bite — the classic NY slice zone. At **70%**, dough becomes softer, more extensible, creates airier crusts with bigger bubbles, but is tackier and requires more experience. At **75%+**, dough is very sticky, spectacular results with enormous blistered pockets, but demands significant handling skill and strong flour.

Flour absorption varies by brand — **Caputo Nuvola at 67.2% feels like Caputo Pizzeria at 65%** in terms of dough consistency. Always adjust for flour type.

### The flour debate in full

**"00" refers to milling fineness, not protein content.** Italy grades flour by ash content; 00 is the finest sift from pure endosperm. Protein ranges from 8–12.5% depending on brand. 00 flour absorbs less water — 65% hydration with 00 feels like ~70% with bread flour.

**For a home oven at 500–550°F, bread flour or high-protein flour is generally superior to 00.** Tony Gemignani states this explicitly: "If I were to make pizza at home in a 500° oven, I would look for a high-gluten, high-protein flour. I wouldn't grab a Caputo or a Neapolitan flour... they don't brown well at 500°." This is because lower-protein 00 flour has less sugar content for Maillard browning and produces a softer crust that doesn't hold up well over longer bake times.

**Specific products the best operators use:**

- **Caputo Pizzeria ("Blue Bag")**: 12.5% protein, W260-270. THE Neapolitan standard for 800–900°F ovens. Not ideal for home ovens.
- **Caputo Saccorosso/Chef's Flour ("Red Bag")**: 13% protein, W300-320. Stronger, designed for longer fermentation and *lower oven temps (500–600°F)*. Best Caputo option for home ovens.
- **King Arthur Bread Flour**: 12.7% protein, unbleached, unbromated. The American workhorse — versatile, affordable (~$5/5lb), excellent for NY-style at home.
- **King Arthur Sir Lancelot Hi-Gluten**: ~14.2% protein. Direct competitor to All Trumps. Professional flour for NY-style and bagels.
- **General Mills All Trumps**: 14.2% protein, high-gluten. THE flour that built New York pizza. Bromated, enriched, contains malted barley. Available only in 50lb bags through distributors.
- **Central Milling Organic Type 00**: Premium artisan mill used by high-end operators. More expensive, prized for flavor.
- **Roberta's blend**: 50/50 Caputo 00 and all-purpose — creates a hybrid crust with Neapolitan char and NY structural integrity. A clever compromise.

**Protein content by style**: Neapolitan needs **11–12.5%**, NY needs **13–14.2%**, Detroit/Sicilian needs **12–14%**.

**Bottom line for the synthesis agent**: King Arthur Bread Flour at 12.7% is the most versatile single flour for a home oven producing neo-Neapolitan/NY hybrid pizza. If the goal is to replicate what Roberta's does, a 50/50 blend of 00 and bread flour bridges both worlds.

### Fermentation: the single biggest quality lever

Cold fermentation science: enzymes break down complex starches into simpler sugars, producing nutty notes, subtle tanginess, and sweetness. These byproducts enhance the Maillard reaction during baking — **insufficient fermentation = fewer amino acids and sugars = pale, bland crust even at high heat.** This is why long-fermented dough browns better. Cold fermentation also strengthens gluten biochemically without aggressive kneading, and makes dough more extensible and easier to stretch.

**Yeast amounts by schedule (instant dry yeast, baker's percentage):**

| Schedule | IDY Amount | Character |
|---|---|---|
| Same-day (4–8 hrs RT) | 0.3–0.5% | Acceptable, one-dimensional |
| 24-hr cold ferment | ~0.12% | Good flavor development |
| 48-hr cold ferment | ~0.06% | Excellent flavor |
| 72-hr cold ferment | ~0.04% | Maximum flavor, best texture |

For 500g flour on a 72-hour cold ferment, that's **~0.2g of instant dry yeast** — requiring a precision scale accurate to 0.01g (~$10–15 on Amazon).

**Equivalence ratio** (PizzaBlab): 1 hour at room temp (68°F) ≈ 4–6 hours in fridge (39°F). Most Naples pizzerias actually ferment at room temperature for 8–24 hours, not cold — cold fermentation is as much a convenience and planning tool as a quality enhancer.

**Flavor development timeline** (Baking Steel): "1 day is good, 2 days is even better, by day 3 the dough is LEGENDARY. Days 4–6 are OK but dough begins to lose vibrancy."

**The fastest path to genuinely great dough**: The Baking Steel same-day recipe uses 67% hydration, doubled yeast (2g per 500g flour), added sugar, 4–6 hours bulk ferment at room temp, ball 3 hours before baking — total ~8 hours. Honest assessment from that source: 72-hour cold ferment is "fine wine," same-day is "cold beer on a hot day" — both have their place. However, even an overnight (12–18 hour) cold ferment dramatically improves results over same-day. The Thursday-night-prep-for-Saturday-service rhythm is popular among operators for good reason.

### No-knead and autolyse

No-knead works well for long fermentation — **time replaces kneading for gluten development.** Method: mix into shaggy mass → autolyse 30 min → stretch and fold 2–3 times during bulk ferment → cold ferment 12–72 hours.

**The windowpane test is irrelevant for long-fermented pizza dough** (PizzaBlab). With fermentation over 8 hours at room temp or over 18 hours in fridge, gluten develops biochemically during fermentation. For short fermentations (<4 hours), the windowpane test IS important.

**Autolyse** (mixing only flour and water, resting 20–30 min before adding salt/yeast) allows flour proteins to hydrate passively, beginning gluten formation. Testing by Crust Kingdom found a 30-minute autolyse produced noticeably smoother dough requiring less kneading, though a non-autolyse dough with 5 minutes of proper kneading performed comparably.

### Dough ball sizing

**The "Rule of 22" (Baking Steel)**: 22 grams per inch of diameter. So a 12" pizza = 264g, a 14" pizza = 308g. Neapolitan standard (AVPN): 200–280g for 10–12" pies. NY standard: 280–380g for 14–16" pies. **250g is the most commonly cited sweet spot** for a standard 12" Neapolitan-style home pizza.

## Sauce, cheese, and the bake: where details compound

### Sauce: raw wins on speed and quality

For a food-truck-minded operation, raw sauce is the clear winner. Anthony Mangieri (Una Pizza Napoletana, ranked #1 pizzeria in the world by 50 Top Pizza 2022) doesn't even add salt to his sauce — just tomatoes. The AVPN standard: hand-crush San Marzano DOP tomatoes, add a pinch of salt. Done in under 5 minutes. Even for home ovens, Baking Steel recommends raw sauce: "Pre-cooking the sauce can dull the fresh, bright flavor."

**Application quantity**: For a 12" pie, **60–80g (about 2 oz / ¼ cup)**. Chef Marc Forgione: "We use two ounces on a 12-inch pie. That should be just enough to cover the bottom and hold the ingredients."

**Critical tip**: Bring sauce to room temperature before spreading — cold sauce on raw dough creates a gummy undercooked layer.

### The tomato brand hierarchy

**Top tier (what elite operators use):**
- **Bianco DiNapoli**: Created by Chris Bianco and Rob DiNapoli. Organically grown in California, steam-peeled, packed in own juices. Named Best Canned Tomato 4 years running by NY Times Wirecutter. ~$5–6 per 28oz can.
- **Stanislaus 7/11 Ground Tomatoes**: THE foodservice secret. California vine-ripened, ground unpeeled — skins add pectin and intense vine-ripe aroma. Available in #10 cans through restaurant supply.
- **Mutti Pomodoro San Marzano**: Italy's #1 brand. Consistently tops blind taste tests. Just tomatoes and sea salt — no calcium chloride or citric acid. ~$3–4 per 28oz can.
- **Cento San Marzano (DOP)**: Canned at their plant near Mt. Vesuvius. Classic Italian profile. ~$4–5 per can.

**The DOP debate**: Multiple blind taste tests have shown DOP San Marzanos don't always win against quality California tomatoes. The DOP label ensures origin and method, not necessarily superiority. **Bianco DiNapoli and Stanislaus compete head-to-head with certified DOP imports.**

**The 30-second test** (Baking Steel): taste a spoonful raw from the can. If it tastes good raw, it'll make incredible sauce.

### Cheese: whole-milk block mozzarella, never pre-shredded

**For NY-style and neo-Neapolitan in a home oven**: Low-moisture, whole-milk mozzarella from a block. **Polly-O** won America's Test Kitchen's blind test — "remarkably soft and milky, with creaminess and hint of salt that drew comparisons to fresh mozzarella." Galbani and BelGioioso are reliable alternatives. Grande Cheese is the professional standard but hard to find retail.

**Whole milk is non-negotiable** — part-skim becomes chewy and tough when cool, lacks creaminess.

**Never use pre-shredded bags** — cellulose anti-caking agents interfere with melting, absorb moisture, and burn easily. Shred fresh from a block on the large holes of a box grater.

**For fresh mozzarella**: tear into small pieces, drain on paper towels for **15–60 minutes minimum**. Mozzarella di bufala has 2× the moisture of cow's milk mozzarella and requires even more aggressive draining.

**Cheese quantity**: For a 12" pie, **140–170g (5–6 oz)**. You should see hints of red sauce through the cheese before baking.

**The NY blend secret**: Many NYC pizzerias use a **50/50 blend of whole-milk and part-skim low-moisture mozzarella** — the blend optimizes melt, browning, stretch, and "holding ability."

### Bake: steel, broiler method, and temperature targets

**Pizza steel decisively outperforms pizza stone** for home ovens. Steel conducts heat **18–20× faster** than ceramic. It heats faster, retains heat better, recovers faster between pies, and is virtually indestructible. Bake time drops from 8–12 minutes (stone at 500°F) to **4–6 minutes (steel at 500°F)**. Kenji endorsed Baking Steel as blowing his favorite stone "out of the water."

**The evolved broiler method (Baking Steel, January 2026 update):**

1. Place steel on second rack from top, 6–7" from broiler element
2. **Electric ovens**: Preheat at **450°F convection** for 1 full hour (not 500°F — electric ovens' safety sensors prevent the broiler from activating if the cavity is already too hot)
3. **Gas ovens**: Preheat at 500°F convection for 1 hour
4. Switch to BROIL on high 5 minutes before launch
5. Launch pizza → broil 90–120 seconds (watch for char) → switch to convection bake → rotate 180° → bake 2–5 more minutes
6. **Total cook time: 3.5–7 minutes, with leopard spotting and puffy cornicione**

**Does a portable pizza oven dramatically change the equation?** Yes. An Ooni or Roccbox reaching **750–850°F** bakes in 60–90 seconds, producing soft charred crust that stays moist inside — a fundamentally different and superior product. The key technique: turn gas to LOWEST setting once pizza is in (high burns the top before bottom cooks), return to high between pizzas, and rotate every 20–30 seconds with a turning peel. Wait for stone to return to 715–850°F before launching the next pie. A $400 Ooni produces results comparable to a $10,000 commercial oven for most purposes.

**Time/temperature relationships:**
- 900°F (wood-fired/Ooni): 60–90 seconds — soft, charred, moist
- 700–800°F: 2–3 minutes — good char, slightly drier
- 550°F (home oven + steel + broiler): 4–6 minutes — crispy bottom, good browning
- 500°F (home oven + stone): 8–12 minutes — acceptable but drier, less oven spring

**Recovery between pies**: Steel needs 5–10 minutes; stone needs 10–15. After 3–4 pies, allow 10–15 minutes of full reheat. **Two-steel method**: place one on top rack under broiler, one on bottom — creates a pizza every 2 minutes by alternating.

## Secret weapon ingredients and where to invest vs. cut corners

### Diastatic malt powder: the single most impactful secret ingredient

Made from sprouted barley, it contains active amylase enzymes that break down starch into sugars — producing **sustained sugar production throughout fermentation** rather than the one-time burst that table sugar provides. Recommended by Tony Gemignani in nearly every dough recipe. Usage: **0.25–2% of flour weight** (½–1 tsp per 3 cups flour for home bakers). Critical nuance: many American flours (King Arthur, All Trumps) already contain malted barley flour. Caputo is unmalted — DMP makes the biggest difference with unmalted flours. Available from King Arthur's website and Amazon; store in freezer.

### Finishing ingredients that elite operators use

Fresh basil torn (not chopped) and added post-bake. Extra virgin olive oil drizzled post-bake — essential for Margherita. **Parmigiano-Reggiano** grated over the top post-bake: a natural glutamate bomb at ~1,200mg per 100g, it amplifies umami dramatically. Some operators sprinkle Parmesan on sauce *under* the mozzarella. Flaky sea salt on the cornicione for textural contrast. Trending: chili oil and hot honey as finishing ingredients at premium pizzerias.

**MSG is uncommon among top operators** — pizza ingredients are already inherently umami-rich (tomatoes ~250mg glutamate/100g, Parmesan ~1,200mg/100g, aged mozzarella, cured meats). The most impactful single umami move: add Parmesan to the sauce.

### Where to invest (biggest impact per dollar)

1. **Cold fermentation time** — free, transforms mediocre dough into exceptional dough
2. **Tomatoes** — the difference between $2 commodity cans and $5 Bianco DiNapoli is immediately obvious on pizza. One can makes 3–4 pies.
3. **Cheese quality** — whole-milk block mozzarella vs. part-skim or pre-shredded is night and day
4. **Baking steel** (~$70–90) — closest to pizzeria results in a home oven

### Where you can cut corners safely

- **Flour**: King Arthur Bread Flour makes excellent pizza. Imported Italian flour is not required.
- **Water**: Filtered tap water is fine. The "NY water" myth is largely debunked. Avoid distilled or reverse-osmosis (strips minerals yeast needs).
- **Salt**: Any fine salt measured by weight works.
- **Yeast**: SAF Red instant from a bulk bag stored in the freezer — cheap, professional standard, lasts 6+ months.

## The food truck lens: operational wisdom for Minessale's Pizza

### How successful operators produce quality at speed

The universal workflow is linear: Dough Prep → Topping Assembly → Oven → Cut & Serve. Pre-portioning is everything — dough balls, sauce in squeeze bottles or portion cups, cheese pre-weighed, toppings pre-chopped in labeled containers. **An inefficient prep table layout adds 30–60 seconds per pizza; over 100 orders, that's an hour of lost service.**

Successful operations assign positions before each shift: one person on dough/skins, one on build, one on oven. Clear verbal communication during rushes is critical. The tight menu is universal — **3–5 pizza options maximum**, allowing speed, reducing waste, and eliminating decision fatigue. Margherita is the food-truck favorite: simple, cost-effective, always a crowd-pleaser.

### Dough pacing is the core operational challenge

Operators pull dough from the fridge **in staggered batches — not all at once**. First batch comes out 2–4 hours before service to warm. Next batch follows 30–45 minutes later. This maintains consistent temperature and proofing across a session. The Lehmann Method (ball dough immediately after kneading, refrigerate straight away) is the professional standard for consistency because it minimizes variables.

**The "fifth pie problem"** — maintaining consistency throughout a session — is solved by this staggering approach, combined with weighing every component, using standardized balling technique, and keeping dough covered at all times (a dry skin on dough balls is the enemy).

### Is store-bought dough ever defensible?

**Trader Joe's dough** (~$1.50–$2/bag) rates a solid 8/10 among enthusiasts. Three varieties; plain is the consensus favorite. Handles well even in 800°F pizza ovens. **Critical tip**: must come to room temp for at least 45–60 minutes (not the 20 min on the package). Contains dough conditioners.

**The honest verdict**: "Good emergency dough" and "not as good as homemade, but only by a little." However, many professional food truck operators actually use pre-made commercial dough balls from suppliers (like Alive & Kickin') — pre-portioned, easy to proof, can be stretched without losing integrity. This is considered a legitimate choice even for quality-focused operations. **A cold-fermented homemade dough ball still blows away any store-bought option**, but if you buy TJ's dough 2–3 days ahead and let it cold-ferment in your own fridge, the gap narrows.

### Equipment that mobile operators actually use

Wood-fired is the dominant choice for artisan trucks: Forno Bravo Viaggio (drop-in for trailers), Marra Forni (rotating deck), and Alfa Pro (reaches 850°F in 30 min, weighs only 363 lbs, produces up to 150 pizzas/hour). For pop-ups and small operations, **Ooni Koda 16** is the most popular — Ooni explicitly supports commercial use through their "Powered by Ooni" program, and multiple Oonis can be stacked for higher throughput. Roccbox (by Gozney) holds heat longer due to better insulation but costs more.

## Pitfalls, non-obvious knowledge, and the science behind why it works

### The ten mistakes that ruin home pizza

1. **Oven not hot enough / insufficient preheat** — the #1 error by a wide margin
2. **Not letting dough reach room temperature** — cold gluten is tight, snaps back, tears. Target **65–75°F** for stretching.
3. **Too much cheese and/or toppings** — creates a moisture barrier that prevents crust from cooking
4. **Using the wrong flour** — all-purpose lacks structure for great pizza
5. **Insufficient fermentation** — rushing the rise = dense, flavorless dough
6. **Too much yeast** — pros use 0.2% for overnight; home cooks routinely use 10× more
7. **Using a rolling pin** — destroys air bubbles, prevents oven spring. The AVPN forbids rolling pins.
8. **Not preheating the baking surface** — the oven air is at temp, but the stone/steel isn't
9. **Too much bench flour** — excess flour changes texture and burns on hot baking surface
10. **Cutting pizza too soon** — rest 2–3 minutes for cheese to set

### Counterintuitive findings the synthesis agent should know

**Overproofed dough is not garbage.** Modernist Cuisine found dough can be resuscitated "up to 10 times before it suffered any serious loss in quality." Degas, reshape, let rise again.

**Preheat at 450°F, not 500°F, for the broiler method.** Electric ovens' safety sensors prevent the broiler from activating if the cavity is already too hot.

**"Lukewarm water" is an oversimplification.** The proper Desired Dough Temperature formula: Water Temp = (DDT × 3) − (Room Temp + Flour Temp + Friction Factor). In summer you may need ice water; in winter, warm water. Target finished dough temp: **73–78°F**.

**The Maillard reaction depends on fermentation quality**, not just oven temperature. Poor/short fermentation = fewer amino acids and sugars = pale, bland crust even at high heat.

**Higher hydration = crispier crust in a home oven** (counterintuitive). More water evaporates during the longer bake, so you need more to start with.

**Parchment paper blocks heat transfer** — pull it out halfway through if you use it for launch, or avoid it entirely. The better launch method: build on the counter, slide the peel under the finished pizza, use semolina as ball bearings.

**The first pizza in a session is often the worst** — oven/steel may not be fully heat-saturated, baker is still calibrating. Make a simple test pizza first or plan the least important pie first.

**Semolina flour is the best peel medium** — coarse granules act as ball bearings, resist moisture absorption longer than white flour. 50/50 semolina and regular flour is the professional blend. Cornmeal works but can burn and create bitter flavor at high temps. Rice flour has the lowest absorption and is Dan Richer's choice.

### Session management for pizza night

Allow **1 dough ball per guest plus 3–5 extra** for mistakes and seconds. Take dough out 1–3 hours before the first bake. Prep all mise en place before the first pie: cheese grated, sauce portioned, toppings sliced. Keep a conventional oven on low (170–200°F) to hold finished pizzas warm. After every 3–4 pies, allow 10–15 minutes for the steel to fully reheat. **Build speed on the peel**: from dough-on-peel to launch should be under 2 minutes or the sauce seeps through and causes sticking.

### The best reheating method (science-backed)

**Cast iron skillet**: place slice in cold skillet, heat over medium-low until bottom is crispy (~2–3 min), add 2–3 drops of water to pan (not on pizza), cover with lid, cook 1–2 more minutes. The skillet crisps the bottom via direct conduction while steam re-melts cheese without drying toppings. Total: 5–6 minutes, nearly as good as fresh.

### The science in brief

**Maillard reaction**: amino acids + reducing sugars at high heat = hundreds of flavor compounds (toasty, nutty, bready). Fermentation produces both the amino acids and sugars required — this is why long-fermented dough browns dramatically better. **Gluten**: glutenin provides elasticity (spring-back), gliadin provides extensibility (stretch without tearing). Resting allows gluten to relax from elastic to extensible. Salt strengthens gluten; fat weakens it. **Oven spring**: CO₂ and steam expand rapidly in high heat, creating the puffy cornicione. Requires properly fermented dough + extremely hot baking surface. **Leopard spotting** (the charred blisters): air bubbles expanding rapidly while sugars caramelize under intense radiant heat. Requires ~800°F+ for the real thing; the broiler method at 550°F achieves a version of it.

## What Minessale's Pizza should probably look like

The synthesis agent has enough data to construct a precise recipe, but the research points strongly toward a specific profile:

A **neo-Neapolitan hybrid** using King Arthur Bread Flour (or a 50/50 bread flour + 00 blend), **65–68% hydration**, **2–2.5% salt**, a tiny amount of instant yeast (~0.1–0.3% depending on fermentation schedule), **1–2% diastatic malt powder or sugar** for home-oven browning, and **2–3% olive oil** for tenderness and extensibility. Cold fermented for **48–72 hours** with minimal effort: mix, fold a few times, ball, refrigerate. Sauce is raw crushed Bianco DiNapoli or quality San Marzano tomatoes with salt and nothing else — 60–80g per 12" pie. Low-moisture whole-milk mozzarella (Polly-O or equivalent), shredded fresh from a block — 140–170g per pie. Baked on a preheated steel under a broiler for 4–6 minutes total. Finished with torn fresh basil, a drizzle of good olive oil, and optionally a dusting of Parmigiano-Reggiano.

**Dough ball weight**: 250–275g for a 12" pie. **Yield**: a 500g flour batch makes 3 pies with minimal waste. The dough requires 10 minutes of hands-on work, 48+ hours of patience, and produces something that genuinely makes people ask where you got it.

The food-truck operational mindset translates directly to home use: prep everything before the first pie, stagger dough balls out of the fridge, keep the menu simple, work fast on the peel, and remember that the quality lives in the fermentation and the heat — not in complexity.
