## 1. CANONICAL IDENTITY: OFFICIAL DEFINITION

### The 1982 Registration (Accademia Italiana della Cucina)



The Accademia Italiana della Cucina's official recipe was notarized and deposited in the Bologna Chamber of Commerce on October 17, 1982.

 

On April 20, 2023, a new updated recipe for the 'real' Ragù alla Bolognese was deposited, replacing the 1982 version.



**Official Ingredient Ratios (1982 Original):**


300 grams beef cartella (thin skirt), 150 grams pancetta, 50 grams carrot, 50 grams celery stalk, 50 grams onion, 5 tablespoons tomato sauce or 20 grams triple tomato extract, 1 cup whole milk, and half cup white or red wine, dry and not frizzante, salt and pepper to taste.



**2023 Updated Ratios:**


The 2023 recipe changed the tomato ratio—instead of 300 grams of tomatoes for 300 grams of beef, the new ratio is roughly 250 grams (200 grams sieved tomatoes plus 1 tablespoon triple concentrate) for 400 grams of beef, making Ragù alla Bolognese even more a beef sauce with tomato, rather than a tomato sauce with beef.



### Key Official Procedure



The pancetta, cut into little cubes and chopped with a mezzaluna chopping knife, is melted in a saucepan; the vegetables are added and everything is left to stew softly. Next the ground beef is added and is left on the stovetop while being stirred constantly, until it sputters. The wine and tomato (cut with a little broth) are added and everything left to simmer for around two hours, adding little by little the milk and adjusting the salt and pepper.



### Distinction from "Spaghetti Bolognese"



Spaghetti bolognese is a popular pasta dish outside Italy, although not part of Italian cuisine. The dish is generally perceived as inauthentic by Italians.

 

Ragù alla bolognese is customarily used to dress tagliatelle al ragù and to prepare lasagne alla bolognese.



### Historical Evolution



The earliest documented recipe for a ragù served with pasta dates back to the end of the 18th century in Imola, near Bologna, from Alberto Alvisi, cook of the local Cardinal. In 1891, Pellegrino Artusi published a recipe for a ragù characterized as bolognese in his cookbook, thought to derive from the mid-19th century.

## 2. KEY TECHNIQUES & FOOD SCIENCE

### SOFFRITTO MASTERY

**Optimal Ratios:**


The soffritto should be half onion, one-quarter celery, and one-quarter carrot. The onion provides most of the flavor, the carrot a bit of sugar, and the celery adds some aromatic vegetable taste.



**Preparation & Browning:**


The vegetables should be finely minced, with all the veggies exactly the same size. Start by julienning the carrot and celery and then finely dice the tiny sticks you made.





Cook the soffritto until it is deeply browned and fragrant, 15-20 minutes. If the vegetables brown too quickly, reduce the heat to medium or medium-low.





It takes a solid 45 minutes to an hour to cook a soffritto properly, but thankfully only a little bit of hovering and stirring. However, the payoff is so worth it. Don't be tempted to crank the heat and brown your vegetables, or cut off the cooking time before they have started to almost disintegrate.



**Food Science Behind Soffritto:**


Browning the vegetables adds depth and leaves fond (flavor-packed browned bits) on the bottom of the pot.

 

This slow caramelization builds the sweet, nutty undertones essential to the sauce's depth of flavor.



**Critical Technique Error:**


Meat and vegetables require different types of cooking. The meat should be well caramelized, so it needs high heat and plenty of agitation, while the vegetables must be browned slowly over low heat.



### MEAT BROWNING & THE MAILLARD REACTION

**Temperature & Technique:**


When ground beef is cooked, the high heat helps break down the proteins and fats, creating a rich, savory flavor referred to as the "Maillard reaction." This is a chemical reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars that gives browned food its distinctive flavor and aroma.





The Maillard reaction occurs at temperatures above 300 degrees F. Meat will not begin to brown until all the surface moisture has evaporated or otherwise been driven off.



**Optimal Browning Practice:**


Get the pan hot—like, smoking hot. Then add the beef, without breaking it up. Despite your instinct to separate the mass, let the beef sit in the hot pan undisturbed for roughly two minutes.





It's essential to break up the meat with a spoon or spatula as it cooks, allowing it to brown evenly and preventing it from forming clumps. You should also avoid stirring the meat too much, as this can prevent it from developing a nice crust on the bottom.



**Meat Fat Content & Quality:**


Beef with a higher fat content, like ground chuck, will brown easier than lean beef. This is because the excess fat renders out as it cooks and begins to fry the fresh ground beef resting in it.





80/20 ground beef is an ideal all-purpose choice. It is the best choice for browning well without adding additional oil.



### DEGLAZING & WINE/MILK ADDITION

**Wine Reduction:**


Slowly pour in the red wine, using a wooden spoon to scrape up any browned bits that may have formed on the bottom of the pan. Simmer until the wine is nearly cooked off, 4-5 minutes.



**Milk's Role in Bolognese:**


Cooking ground beef in milk is a traditional step in classic Bolognese—and there's solid science behind it. Milk gently tenderizes the meat thanks to its calcium and mild acidity. As Harold McGee explains in On Food and Cooking, calcium can activate natural enzymes in meat that help break down proteins, softening the texture over time without making it mushy. The milk's fat and proteins also add moisture and richness, giving the sauce its signature velvety feel.



**Modern Testing on Milk:**


López-Alt ran a little experiment with his Bolognese and concluded, "Turns out, the liquid you cook the meat in has absolutely no bearing on how tender the result. Meat cooked in stock is indistinguishable from meat cooked in milk. That said, adding milk to the cooking liquid and letting it reduce does have an effect on the finished flavor of the ragù, giving it a more rounded profile."



### SLOW SIMMERING: TIMING & TRANSFORMATIONS

**Minimum Simmering Time:**


Add the whole milk and simmer until completely evaporated, then stir in freshly grated nutmeg, followed by white wine. Simmer until evaporated, then stir in canned chopped tomatoes and their juices and simmer very gently for 3 hours, stirring occasionally.





Hazan's original calls for simmering the sauce for 3 to 5 hours; if you want to cook it longer, Hazan wrote that it can go up to 7 hours.



**Why Extended Simmer Time Matters:**


This sauce gets its incredible flavor from 3 to 4 hours of simmer time, which produces a rich, intensely flavorful sauce.





This isn't something you want to rush. This isn't something you want to throw into your pressure cooker (though people have tried). While it might be good, it's not great. That rich umami flavor that has you wanting to just eat the sauce straight out of a pan with a spoon takes some time.



**Texture & Consistency Evolution:**


The sauce should reduce until thickened, to the point where a wooden spoon should leave a trail when dragged through the sauce, at around 1½ hours.

## 5. COMMON MISTAKES & WHY THEY FAIL

### **Rushing the Simmer**



The secret to Bolognese is the simmering. You can't rush this process.





The most common flaw of quick Bolognese recipes is that they were too tomatoey and acidic—more like meaty marinara than true Bolognese, which actually contains relatively little tomato. Most also lacked the traditional sauce's velvety consistency and ultrasavory flavor; in other words, they tasted like the shortcut sauces they were.



### **Excessive Tomato**



Quick Bolognese recipes were too tomatoey and acidic—more like meaty marinara than true Bolognese, which actually contains relatively little tomato.



### **Wrong Pasta Pairing (Spaghetti)**



Unfortunately, spaghetti is too slippery and sauce will not adhere to it, and it is never used in Bologna with a ragu. Seriously, it's almost like an unwritten law.





There is no such thing as spaghetti Bolognese in Italy. The sauce slides right off the circular shaped spaghetti noodles, leaving you with a pile of plain pasta and a bowl full of sauce at the bottom.



**Why Tagliatelle is Superior:**


Tagliatelle represents the perfect middle ground between pappardelle and fettuccine. This is the traditional pasta for Bolognese sauce—the width perfectly balances the meat and tomato components.





Since tagliatelle is typically made fresh, the pasta noodles are a bit sticky and porous allowing the meat sauce to cling to every bite.



### **Insufficient Browning**



While it may be tempting to keep all those meat juices in your sauce, they actually produce less flavor than letting them evaporate, so the meat can develop some roasted texture of its own.





The second reason to brown ground beef is to create flavor using the Maillard reaction, chemical reactions that create richer, deeper flavors in the meat. You will notice a fond developing on the bottom of your pan. When you add the liquid, all that fond gets mixed in, creating even more flavor.



### **Omitting Dairy**



The first is to soften the tartness and acidity of the tomatoes. At the same time, it brings a subtle creaminess to the sauce, which might otherwise be described as a velvety finish.

## 9. FOOD SCIENCE CONSENSUS

### **Why Extended Simmering Works**

Extended simmering accomplishes three key transformations:
1. **Collagen breakdown**: Tough cuts of meat contain collagen that breaks down into gelatin, contributing to the sauce's silky texture
2. **Flavor concentration**: Water evaporates, concentrating flavors and deepening umami
3. **Texture mellowing**: Meat fibers become tender and break down into a cohesive sauce

### **The Maillard Reaction's Critical Role**



The Maillard reaction creates brown pigments in cooked meat in a very specific way: by rearranging amino acids and certain simple sugars. The important thing about the Maillard reaction isn't the color, it's the flavors and aromas. Indeed, it should be called "the flavor reaction," not the "browning reaction." The molecules it produces provide the potent aromas responsible for the characteristic smells of roasting, baking, and frying.

## PART 1: COMMON MISTAKES & PROFESSIONAL TIPS

### **CRITICAL MISTAKES THAT SEPARATE MEDIOCRE FROM EXCELLENT**

#### **1. Rushing the Simmer (Most Common & Most Damaging)**



There is no quick version of ragù—it's a recipe that requires three hours of cooking plus the time to prepare vegetables

. 

The main issue people face when making Bolognese is improper simmering time or temperature; a slow, steady simmer allows the flavors to meld together, while cooking it too fast leads to a watery, less flavorful sauce

.

**Pro Tip:** 

When the tomatoes begin to bubble, turn the heat down so that the sauce cooks at the laziest of simmers, with just an intermittent bubble breaking through to the surface

. 

The long, gentle simmer allows flavors to concentrate through evaporation while the meat becomes tender and the sauce thickens naturally

.

#### **2. Overcrowding the Pan During Meat Browning**



Overcrowding the pan is another mistake that can ruin your Bolognese—when there's too much meat in the pan, it can't brown properly; instead of searing, the meat steams in its own juices, resulting in a less flavorful sauce

.

**Pro Tip:** 

To get that beautiful brown color, cook the meat in smaller batches; this way, each piece has room to sear—it might take longer, but it will make a significant difference in flavor

. 

Let the meat sit undisturbed for a minute or two between stirs so it develops a nice brown crust—this Maillard reaction creates incredible depth of flavor; you want deep caramelization, not just gray cooked meat

.

#### **3. Improperly Browning Soffritto vs. Meat**



Meat and vegetables need different types of cooking—the meat should be caramelized, so it needs a lively fire, while vegetables must brown slowly over low heat; so use two pans and then combine them all into one

.

**Pro Tip:** 

Make sure the soffritto (the onions, carrots, and celery) doesn't brown—that way, you get sweetness and depth without introducing bitter notes

. 

Cook vegetables over low heat, stirring occasionally, until softened and translucent, about 10-15 minutes; this slow cooking process, called sweating, is essential for developing the flavors of the soffritto; avoid browning the vegetables

.

#### **4. Adding Salt at the Wrong Time**



Salt helps extract water from food; if added too soon, it could make everything burn—during the cooking of vegetables, a lot of water is released; if salt is added immediately, the water is extracted too quickly and the vegetables risk burning, giving the sauce a horrible taste

.

**Pro Tip:** 

Adding salt at the beginning can cause the sauce to become overly salty after hours of simmering and flavor concentration; add salt just before finishing the sauce, once the flavors have melded, to ensure a well-balanced seasoning

.

#### **5. Omitting or Skipping Dairy (Milk)**



Milk is an essential ingredient in ragù; it must be added a little at a time, at the end; the quantity varies according to the quantity of meat used; add a little at a time and mix the meat well, until the milk is completely absorbed by the sauce; reduce for 20 minutes and then your ragù is ready

.

**Pro Tip:** 

Whole milk is a traditional ingredient in classic bolognese—milk tenderizes the meat and produces a richer, more flavorful sauce

. 

Simmering the browned meat in milk before adding the tomatoes is a game-changer; it results in a texture that is unbelievably tender and melts in your mouth

.

#### **6. Using Excessive Tomato (Too Tomatoey)**



Ragù alla Bolognese should always be a beef sauce with tomato, rather than a tomato sauce with beef—this has become even more true in the 2023 official recipe, as the amount of tomato in relation to the beef has been decreased quite a bit

.

**Pro Tip:** 

Authentic Bolognese is a slow-cooked meat sauce; authentic Bolognese uses tomato paste (not crushed tomatoes) for concentrated tomato flavor without excess liquid; the sauce should be meat-forward, not tomato-forward

.

#### **7. Wrong Wine or Poor-Quality Wine**



Common mistakes include using low-quality wine, rushing the vegetable soffritto, and skipping the milk addition; these shortcuts prevent the sauce from developing the depth that makes authentic bolognese special

.

**Pro Tip:** 

Wine is more than a liquid in Bolognese—it's an aromatic enhancer that deepens the sauce's complexity; using a poor-quality or fruity wine can leave your sauce flat or overly sweet; opt for a dry white wine like Sauvignon Blanc or Pinot Grigio

.

### **PROFESSIONAL TECHNIQUES FOR EXCELLENCE**

#### **Soffritto Mastery**



Soffritto is the flavour base of every good Bolognese sauce; it's simply the mixture of finely diced onion, celery, carrot, garlic and herbs; sautéed in olive oil until soft with just a hint of caramelisation, a good soffritto imparts incredible flavour to the minced meat, tomato and stock once they are added to the pot

.



Finely dice the onion, carrot, and celery; the smaller the dice, the better the vegetables will melt into the sauce

.

#### **Multi-Stage Browning for Complexity**



Most people brown the meat once and move on, but browning it in three stages—first the meat alone, then with soffritto, then again after adding tomato paste—creates different flavor compounds that can't be replicated in a single browning

.

#### **Deglazing Technique**



Slowly pour in the red wine, using a wooden spoon to scrape up any browned bits that may have formed on the bottom of the pan; simmer until the wine is nearly cooked off, 4-5 minutes

.

#### **Monitoring Consistency**



Drag your wooden spoon across the bottom of the pot; if it leaves a clear trail that takes a second or two to fill back in, you're in the perfect zone; if it fills in instantly, it's too thin; if it's so thick you're basically just pushing meat around, it's too thick

.
