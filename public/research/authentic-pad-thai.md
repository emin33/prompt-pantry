## **SECTION 1: CANONICAL IDENTITY**

### **1.1 Origin Story & Cultural Significance**



Pad Thai was invented in the 1940s, under the administration of nationalist prime minister Plaek Phibunsongkhram, who organized a competition to create a dish that could represent the nation.

 

The popularisation of Pad Thai and its establishment as the national dish of Thailand is attributed to Plaek Phibunsongkhram (Prime Minister of Thailand 1938 – 1944, 1948 – 1957). Part of Phibunsongkhram's nation-building strategy was to develop 'Thai-ness' and impose a 'Thai Great Tradition' to demonstrate the strength and unity of the Thai nation. His series of decrees from 1939–1942 suggested what could be done to strengthen the Thai economy, to instill national image and pride—and to improve the national diet.





In 1942, during World War II, a flood seriously damaged the rice paddies in Chao Phraya River Delta, including Bangkok, for three months, causing the government to launch a campaign to encourage people to trade and eat noodles.

 

One bowl of rice could be used to make two bowls of rice noodles, so Phibun touted noodles as an alternative starch in hopes of stretching the nation's rice supply.

 

As part of a national campaign called "Noodle is Your Lunch," the Public Welfare Department gave Thais free noodle carts and distributed recipes for a new national dish: pad Thai.



**Key Context for Recipe Development:** 

competitions were held in schools, government offices and various nationalistic organisations to devise new noodle recipes, including the winning one that included tamarind and palm sugar. It was given the name pat thai, in keeping with the chauvinistic tenor of the times, and to distinguish it from Chinese noodle dishes, even though it has much in common with them – bean sprouts, bean curd, salted radish, garlic chives and, of course, the noodles themselves.



### **1.2 Defining Characteristics: The Four Flavor Pillars**



The sauce's job is to deliver the four fundamental Thai flavor pillars in a single concentrated liquid: sweet, sour, salty, and umami.

 Achieving true authenticity requires understanding each element:

- **Sweetness:** 

Palm Sugar (น้ําตาลปี๊บ, nam tan bpeep in Thai from the borassus flabellifera palm) has a rich caramel flavor quite different from cane sugar.


- **Sourness:** 

What distinguishes Pad Thai sauce from other Thai sauces — or any other Asian stir-fry sauce is the central role of tamarind. In Pad Thai, it comes from tamarind, a fruit with a distinctly deeper, fruitier, almost date-like tartness that vinegar can't replicate. That tartness, combined with the caramel sweetness of palm sugar and the complex salinity of fish sauce, creates the flavor profile that makes Pad Thai immediately recognizable.


- **Texture:** 

Thin, flat, quite chewy rice noodles are preferred here: fresh ones make a much better dish, but they are hard to find outside of Thailand.



### **1.3 Authentic vs. Americanized Versions**



There is plenty of bad pad Thai out there – many versions are too sweet or ketchupy. A true pad Thai, however, is a thing of beauty – and can even be made at home.

 

Contemporary versions, particularly in the U.S., tend toward big flavor—more heat, more salt, more acidity, and, above all, more sweetness than Phibunsongkhram's elegant diplomatic dish. Funky or fermented ingredients like dried shrimp and salted radish are often dialed back or replaced with soy sauce or table salt.





What does NOT belong in authentic Pad Thai sauce: Ketchup, peanut butter, hoisin sauce, oyster sauce, or rice vinegar as primary sour components. These are all Western adaptations or cross-cuisine shortcuts. Some will produce edible results; none will produce Pad Thai that tastes like Thailand.



**Expert Authority:** 

David Thompson could be the most famous non-Thai Thai chef in the world. His version of the stir-fried noodles bore no resemblance to any I'd tasted before. It was salty, sour and slightly sweet. Thompson's credo is that the diner should finish the dish by adding more lime, fish sauce or chilies as they desire.

## **SECTION 2: KEY TECHNIQUES**

### **2.1 Wok Hei Development: The Science of "Breath of the Wok"**

Wok hei is not mystical—it is measurable food chemistry. 

The distinctive flavors and appealing appearance of authentic Asian stir-fry depend heavily on Maillard reactions—the complex chemical processes that create browning, develop savory flavors, and contribute to the legendary "wok hei." These reactions occur when amino acids and reducing sugars interact under specific temperature conditions, producing hundreds of different flavor compounds.



**Temperature Requirements:** 

While Maillard reactions occur at relatively moderate temperatures (250-300°F), successful wok cooking requires oil temperatures of 400°F or higher to ensure food surfaces reach the Maillard temperature range quickly while maintaining proper texture and moisture control.

 More precisely, 

Wok hei requires temperatures between 650-750°F (340-400°C), far beyond what most home stoves can achieve.



**Home Cooking Strategies:** 

Wok hei recipe mastery isn't about owning a jet engine burner; it is the scientific art of capturing "the breath of the wok" through the precise combustion of aerosolized oil and the Maillard reaction at temperatures above 300°C (572°F).



**Critical Moisture Management:** 

The biggest enemy of a great wok hei recipe is moisture. When you crowd the pan, the temperature drops instantly. Instead of searing, the food releases water, and suddenly, you are steaming your beef in its own juices. We call this the "Steam Bath," and it kills flavor. To get wok hei, the food must remain dry enough to fry. My Rule of Thumb: Never fill the wok more than 1/3 full.



**Wok Material Science:** 

Most experts attribute it to three main factors: the aroma compounds that are created with searingly hot oil, the chemical interactions between the food and the wok's surface, and the ultra-fast browning and caramelisation that takes place (otherwise known as the Maillard reaction). In practice, this means wok hei relies on having a well-seasoned wok (preferably carbon steel or cast iron), coupled with the firepower of a commercial wok burner. The rest is all about timing and technique: adding the oil and ingredients at the right time, the continuous 'wok tossing' motion to control the level of heat and moisture, and cooking the ingredients just long enough to impart that signature wok flavour while also retaining their texture.



**Flavor Compound Profile:** 

Pyrazines: Create nutty, roasted flavors characteristic of properly browned proteins. Furans: Contribute caramel-like sweetness that balances savory elements. Thiazoles: Provide meaty, umami characteristics that enhance overall flavor depth.



### **2.2 Noodle Preparation**

**Soaking Science:** 

To get the noodles right, we soaked rice sticks in hot tap water for 20 minutes before stir-frying for tender but not sticky noodles.

 

Soaking rice noodles in boiling water, versus cooking them in the hot water, prevented the delicate strands from getting too soft.



**Thompson's Method:** 

If using dried noodles, soak them in water for about 15 minutes until soft but not overly so. Meanwhile, bring a pan of water to the boil. Drain the noodles well then blanch them in the boiling water for a moment only and drain once again (this prevents the noodles from clumping together when they are stir-fried).



**Andy Ricker's Technique:** 

In a medium bowl, add the cut noodles and enough lukewarm water to cover them by 1 inch. Set aside to soak until very pliable, about 30 minutes.

 

Semi-dried, flat rice noodles, which are available online and in many Asian markets, are worth seeking out, as they hold their shape and texture better than the fully dried version.



**Kenji López-Alt's Approach:** 

Begin by soaking your rice noodles for at least 20 minutes if they are dry.



### **2.3 Sauce Emulsification & Reduction**



We don't cook the noodles like you cook pasta, we sauté them in a pan of sauce. The idea is to cook the noodles until they absorb enough sauce to get the flavor right—but they also need to absorb enough liquid to get soft.



**Hot Thai Kitchen Method (Caramelization Technique):** 

Chop the palm sugar finely. Add the sugar to a small pot and let it melt over medium heat. Let the sugar caramelize into a dark brown colour then immediately add water to stop the caramelization (it will bubble aggressively) Add fish sauce and tamarind paste, then turn off the heat.



**Science of Caramelization:** 

Caramelizing the palm sugar adds a unique, pleasing flavor.

 This Maillard-like reaction in the sauce creates depth that simple mixing cannot achieve.

### **2.4 Aromatic Blooming**



Add the tofu, radish, and toasted dried shrimp to an empty area of the wok.

 

Before you use dried shrimp for phat thai or papaya salad, you must first briefly soak and rinse the crustaceans and then slowly toast them in a dry pan until they're aromatic and dry once more, this time with a chewy-crisp texture. Soak the shrimp in a small bowl full of water for 10 minutes. Drain, briefly rinse, and pat them dry. Set a small dry skillet or flat-bottomed wok over medium heat. Add the shrimp and cook, stirring frequently, until they are dry all the way through and slightly crispy, 8 to 10 minutes.



### **2.5 Egg Incorporation**

**Andy Ricker's Method (Two-Stage Technique):** 

Use the wok spatula to flip the egg (it's okay if the yolk breaks) and cook, without stirring, for 1 minute. Use the spatula to break the egg into several rough pieces, then stir everything together vigorously and continuously for 1 minute more.



**High-Heat Execution:** 

When wok is smoking hot, add the egg and let it cook undisturbed. Immediately add shrimp, followed by tofu, dried shrimp, and salted radish. Use metal spatula to break up egg yolk and turn everything over to sear.



### **2.6 Protein Cooking & Timing**

**Shrimp Specifics:** 

Add the noodles on top of the ingredients in the wok and cook, without stirring, for 45 seconds. (The moisture clinging to the noodles will steam up dramatically.) Vigorously stir everything together, then add ¼ cup plus 2 tablespoons of the prepared sauce, and stir again. Add the bean sprouts and cook, stirring occasionally, until the noodles are fully tender and have absorbed the sauce, and the bean sprouts are tender but still crunchy, 1–1½ minutes.

### **3.2 Understanding Tamarind Variation & Sourcing**

**Critical Issue—Tamarind Intensity:**


If using tamarind paste (especially homemade), it is diluted with water and usually has a more mild flavor. Store-bought tamarind concentrate can vary widely in intensity and sourness. Either way, make sure you taste the sauce and adjust to fit your preferences.





Make sure you buy a product of Thailand, not India, and it should be a brown, pourable liquid.



**Why Indian Tamarind Fails:** 

Do not use Indian tamarind concentrate (thick, black paste) — it is far more concentrated and will make the sauce unpleasantly sour.



**Preparation from Block (Home-Made):** 

Break off roughly 50g of tamarind block pulp and place in a small bowl. Pour over ½ cup of just-boiled water. Let soak for 5 minutes, then use your fingers to break up the pulp and dissolve it in the water. Press the mixture through a fine-mesh strainer, pushing firmly to extract as much liquid as possible. Discard the seeds and fibrous solids. What passes through is your tamarind paste.



### **3.3 Flavor Balancing Protocol**



Taste the sauce. The flavor should hit all four notes simultaneously: you should taste sweetness, then sourness, then the salty-umami depth. None of the three should dominate. Adjust: too sour? Add palm sugar a teaspoon at a time. Too sweet? Add fish sauce a teaspoon at a time. Too salty? Add more tamarind paste or a small squeeze of lime juice. Flat overall? A small amount of each — the sauce needs all three in balance to taste dimensional. Tamarind sourness varies significantly between brands and batches, so this step is necessary every time.



### **3.4 Sauce per Serving**



Standard serving ratio for Pad Thai: Use approximately 3 tablespoons of sauce per single serving of Pad Thai (about 100g dry noodles). For 2 servings, 5–6 tablespoons. For 4 servings (a full large-batch wok), use the entire recipe above.

## **SECTION 6: EQUIPMENT OPTIMIZATION FOR HOME COOKING**

### **6.1 Wok Type & Seasoning**



Traditional Chinese woks are made from carbon steel, which is an ideal material for wok hei due to its high heat conductivity, durability, and non-stick properties when seasoned. When choosing a wok for home cooking, look for one made from carbon steel or cast iron, as these materials retain heat well and can achieve the high temperatures needed for wok hei.



**Critical Limitation:** 

If you are using a Teflon or non-stick coated pan, stop. You will never achieve wok hei. Worse, heating Teflon to the temperatures required for this technique can release toxic fumes.



### **6.2 High Heat Source Strategy**



In professional Chinese kitchens, wok hei is achieved using specialized equipment and techniques honed through years of practice. Jet Burners: Commercial wok ranges produce 100,000-160,000 BTUs (compared to home stoves at 7,000-15,000 BTUs)



**Electric Stove Workaround:** 

Yes, absolutely. The stove type matters less when you use the "Torch Hei" method because the torch provides the combustion flavor. However, electric stoves recover heat slowly. To compensate, cook in even smaller batches (half a cup of ingredients at a time) to ensure the pan stays hot.



### **6.3 Non-Wok Alternatives**

**America's Test Kitchen's Skillet Method:** 

Heat 1 tablespoon oil in 12-inch skillet (preferably nonstick) over high heat until just beginning to smoke, about 2 minutes.

 This proves pad thai is achievable without a wok, though with less dramatic wok hei development.

# COMPREHENSIVE PAD THAI RESEARCH

## 1. MISTAKES & TIPS: Common Pitfalls & Professional Solutions

### **CRITICAL MISTAKES THAT SEPARATE MEDIOCRE FROM EXCELLENT**

#### **A. Noodle Texture Failures**



Many home cooks mistakenly use the wrong type of noodles or overcook them, leading to a mushy, clumpy mess.

 The technical issues and fixes:

- **Oversoak/overcooked noodles:** 

If noodles are soaked in hot off-the-boil water, they must soak for exactly 3 minutes and be placed in cold water immediately after. If over soaked they will quickly become too soft.

 The proper method: 

Dry ones need to soak for about an hour in room-temperature water (never boil).



- **Wrong noodle width:** 

Go with medium-width noodles (about 1/4 inch). They hold sauce without falling apart.

 

If noodles are too thin (vermicelli), they'll dissolve. If too thick, they won't absorb the sauce correctly.



- **Clumping prevention:** 

Drain the noodles well then blanch them in boiling water for a moment only and drain once again (this prevents the noodles from clumping together when they are stir-fried).



#### **B. Unbalanced Sauce—The #1 Flavor Mistake**



Think of sauce like a three-legged stool. Sweet, sour, salty – all need to be equal or it tips over.

 Home cooks typically fail here:

- **Not tasting and adjusting:** 

Taste as you go. Trust your tongue more than measurements.

 Professional fix: 

Taste as you go. Is it too sour? Add a pinch more palm sugar. Too salty? More lime juice. Real-time feedback loops are just as vital in the kitchen as they are in your A/B testing.



- **Too sweet (most common Western mistake):** 

Contemporary versions, particularly in the U.S., tend toward big flavor—more heat, more salt, more acidity, and, above all, more sweetness than Phibunsongkhram's elegant diplomatic dish. Funky or fermented ingredients like dried shrimp and salted radish are often dialed back or replaced with soy sauce or table salt.

 Solution: 

Increase the acidity. A double squeeze of lime or a teaspoon of extra tamarind will cut right through that sugar.



- **Using tamarind incorrectly:** 

Tamarind provides a deep, fruity tartness that lime juice lacks. Lime is for finishing; tamarind is for the soul.



#### **C. Lack of Wok Hei (Breath of the Wok) & Flavor Depth**



Wok hei (breath of the wok) adds smoky umami that home cooks often miss. High heat is crucial for developing these complex flavors.

 Pro method: 

Stir-frying at high heat is essential for an authentic Pad Thai. A hot wok ensures that ingredients cook quickly, retaining their texture and flavor.



- **Crowding the wok (causes temperature crash):** 

Cook no more than 2-3 servings at a time to avoid crowding the pan and making the noodles soggy.

 Chef's protocol: 

If you throw in the shrimp, tofu, eggs, and noodles all at once, the temperature drops. Cook your protein first, set it aside, then bring it back at the end.



- **Losing the saucy/wet texture problem:** 

A perfect Pad Thai is not wet or saucy. The noodles should absorb the sauce completely, leaving them glistening but not swimming in liquid. If your plate has a puddle at the bottom, you've over-indexed on the wrong metrics.



#### **D. Ingredient Substitutions & Authenticity Failures**



One thing many poor versions have in common is a peculiar red tint to the sauce-coated noodles. The culprit? Ketchup. It's an ingredient traditional pad Thai doesn't use.

 Why it fails: 

While ketchup offers much of the flavors combined in pad Thai — sweet, tangy, and with natural umami from tomatoes — it's no substitute for authentic ingredients and can't conjure the same depth of sophisticated and nuanced flavor.





Make sure you purchase a tamarind from Thailand. I've tried using tamarind from India and it does not taste the same.



#### **E. Aromatic Blooming Errors**



Great Thai dishes depend on aroma before they rely on flavor. Beginners frequently sauté lemongrass, galangal, or shallots too early or over too much flame, burning off their volatile oils before vegetables and proteins ever enter the pan.

 Correct technique: 

Thai cooking expects fast but smart heat — not simply high heat. The ingredients must bloom, not scorch. Heat should briefly kiss the aromatics so the oil captures and carries the fragrance forward into the dish.



#### **F. Egg Incorporation Mistakes**



One of the biggest mistakes of cooking stir fry — including pad Thai — is scrambling the egg at the beginning and leaving it in the pan with the other ingredients. Unfortunately, this method results in overcooked, rubbery eggs lacking that gorgeous buttery softness.

 Professional method: 

Push the noodles to the side of the wok, add a drop of oil, and scramble the eggs in the empty space before folding them back in.



#### **G. Missing Umami & Textural Elements**



Skipping salted preserved radish and dried shrimp is a mistake. These are the "micro-optimizations" of your dish. They provide tiny pops of flavor and texture that the average person can't name but would notice if they were gone.

 

It should have lots of "bits" which gives flavour and textural complexity: Aside from beansprouts it should also have shallots, garlic, tofu, dried shrimp, peanuts, and garlic chives. If it's just a plate of 90% noodles, it is not good pad thai!



### **PRO TIPS FOR EXCELLENCE**

#### **Mise en Place & Batching**



Never double the recipe in one pan. Scale by cooking in batches.

 

Maximizing the ingredients' contact with the wok is essential, so chef Andy Ricker advises against doubling this recipe. If you are cooking for a group, take a cue from Thai noodle vendors and batch out however many servings you'd like to prepare in advance.



#### **Sauce Viscosity Control**



Thin sauce distributes better than thick. Should coat a spoon lightly, not gloop off it. Room temperature sauce mixes easier than cold. Cold sauce clumps when it hits hot wok.



#### **Wok Selection for Home Cooks**



At home, the best substitute is a large cast iron skillet. It retains high heat, lets the noodles sear beautifully, and gives that smoky "street food" flavor you get in Thailand. If you don't have cast iron, use a large stainless-steel or carbon-steel wok instead of nonstick so you can get real browning on the noodles.

## 2. AUTHENTICITY & REGIONAL VARIATIONS

### **CANONICAL IDENTITY: The Historical Context**

#### **Prime Minister Plaek Phibunsongkhram's Role**



During World War II, Thailand suffered a rice shortage due to the war and floods. To reduce domestic rice consumption, the Thai government under Prime Minister Plaek Phibunsongkhram promoted consumption of noodles instead. His government promoted rice noodles and helped to establish the modern identity of Thailand. As a result, a new noodle dish called sen chan pad thai (named after Chanthaburi Province) was created.



More significantly: 

Phibun made it his mission to create a unified national identity that would bring his people together. His efforts included changing the name of the country from Siam to Thailand, introducing a new national anthem, and promoting a new national dish: pad thai.

 

As part of a national campaign called "Noodle is Your Lunch," the Public Welfare Department gave Thais free noodle carts and distributed recipes for a new national dish: pad Thai.



#### **Chinese Origins (Contested But Significant)**



Another explanation by Thai-American food writer Kasma Loha-unchit suggests that pad thai was invented by the Thai Chinese immigrants, because "for a dish to be so named in its own country clearly suggests an origin that isn't Thai". Noodle cookery in most Southeast Asian countries was introduced by the wave of immigrants from southern China settling in the region the past century.



### **DEFINING CHARACTERISTICS: Essential Flavor & Texture**

#### **Foundational Flavor Profile**



Pad Thai is Thailand's most famous noodle dish, tangy, sweet, salty, and savory all in one. It's made with rice noodles, tamarind concentrate, fish sauce, palm sugar, garlic chives, bean sprouts, your choice of protein (often shrimp in Thailand), chicken, and/or tofu, and topped with crushed peanuts, Thai chili flakes and a squeeze of lime.





Thompson's version was salty, sour and slightly sweet. Thompson's credo is that the diner should finish the dish by adding more lime, fish sauce or chilies as they desire.



#### **Texture & Component Balance**



When properly cooked, all the sauce should be absorbed into the noodles. This also allows ingredients to toast and deepen the flavour. The noodles should be lightened with a LOT of bean sprouts. Thai people don't like things to be heavy, so the noodles should be laced with plenty of bean sprouts, AND there should also be extra served on the side for extra freshness.

### **AUTHENTIC INGREDIENTS & RATIOS**

#### **The Sacred Trinity (Tamarind, Palm Sugar, Fish Sauce)**



Pad Thai sauce is a mix of tamarind paste, fish sauce, and palm sugar, the classic Thai trinity that gives Pad Thai its perfect harmony of sweet, sour, and savory. I also add brown sugar for a bit more depth and water to mellow it out.





The ratio is 1:1:1, which makes it easier for you to prepare this sauce in bigger or smaller quantities. You just need to make sure that all the ingredients are perfectly balanced.



#### **Specific Sauce Ratios from Authorities**

Andy Ricker's authoritative ratio:
- 

3 tablespoons tamarind water (naam makham) OR 2 Tbsp tamarind pulp
- 2 tablespoons plus ¾ tsp palm sugar simple syrup (Naam Cheuam Naam Taan Piip), heat equal parts palm sugar and water until palm sugar dissolves
- 1 tablespoon medium-size dried shrimp, rinse, pat dry, then dry-fry in a skillet over medium heat until crispy, 5 minutes





In a small pot over medium heat, combine ¾ cup tamarind paste, ¾ cup palm sugar, ¾ cup brown sugar, and ⅓ to ½ cup fish sauce. Stir until the sugars fully dissolve and everything is smooth.



#### **Tamarind: The Non-Negotiable Component**



Mainstream Pad Thai in the U.S. is often a super sweet, red-hued concoction involving lots of ketchup. Traditionally, the tangy sweetness of pad Thai comes from sugar and tamarind paste.

 Critical distinction: 

There's nothing like the pulp. Both tamarind concentrate from a jar and tamarind pulp work, but definitely get the tamarind pulp if you can find it. The difference in flavor was significant.



#### **Palm Sugar Authenticity**



This is the original sweetener used in Thailand. It has a sweet, caramel flavor that is more nuanced than white sugar.

 

Palm sugar provides a deeper, more caramel-like sweetness than brown sugar. Fish sauce is non-negotiable for its salty, umami depth.



#### **Dried Shrimp (Critical Umami)**



Andy Ricker uses dried shrimp in Pad Thai, which gives Pad Thai an underlying savory flavor.

 

If you truly hate the idea of using dried shrimp, you can experiment with adding 1 tablespoon of oyster sauce instead. That said, like an anchovy in a pasta sauce, the shrimp melds into the dish and adds a great umami flavor.



### **AUTHENTIC vs. COMMON WESTERN ADAPTATIONS**



This won't be like the syrupy, wet takeout with clumpy noodles. You'll get properly wok-fried rice noodles lightened with beansprouts, and a complex-yet-balanced flavour of sweet, salty and sour.





Most bottled pad thai sauces are overly sweet and use cheap ingredients that compromise the flavour. Instead of palm sugar they use granulated white sugar. Instead of fish sauce they add salt and maybe even soy sauce which is not the right flavour. And instead of using all tamarind they will mix in white vinegar or rice vinegar which is much cheaper.



### **REGIONAL VARIATIONS IN THAILAND**

#### **North vs. Buddhist Traditions**



Even in Thailand, regional and religious dietary preferences mean no single "authentic" pad thai recipe exists. The more carnivorous north adds ground pork, while Buddhist Thais prepare a light, vegan version, omitting alliums and chiles too.



#### **Pad Thai Boran (Ancient/Traditional Style)**



In Thailand, fresh banana blossom is sometimes served on the side of pad thai. (Some versions feature this variation, though not all cooks favor it.)



#### **David Thompson's Street Vendor Version**



There is now a gentrified version of pat thai that uses fresh prawns. If you want to stroll along boulevards rather than trawl the alleys, then add six medium-sized cleaned raw prawns as the shallots begin to fry – and omit the dried prawns called for later in the recipe.



This reflects a key distinction: the "street version" (traditional, uses dried shrimp) versus the "modern/upscale" version (fresh shrimp).

#### **Contemporary Southern Thai Variations**



Regional variations developed. For example, in southern Thailand, some cooks add pickled radish, while others might use crab instead of shrimp. Today, you can find Pad Thai with many creative variations, including vegan or vegetarian options for international audiences.



### **WHAT PURISTS INSIST ON**



Most Thais don't make their own Pad Thai sauce because they usually grab Pad Thai from a street vendor! But as long as the sauce has tamarind, palm sugar, and fish sauce, you know it's legit.





The wok-frying and noodles reflect the dish's Chinese influence, but the components that define its distinct flavor—sweet-and-sour tamarind, funky fish sauce, and palm sugar—are characteristically Thai.
