# Raw research findings for exceptional gluten-free pizza dough

**Gluten-free pizza dough that credibly mimics a neo-Neapolitan pie baked on steel under a broiler is achievable, but almost every parameter of the original wheat recipe must change.** The hydration jumps from 66.7% to 75–85%, the diastatic malt must be replaced entirely, the dough cannot be hand-stretched, and a par-bake step becomes nearly mandatory. The consensus gold-standard flour is Caputo Fioreglut, and the consensus gold-standard binder is psyllium husk powder. A puffy cornicione is possible. Cold fermentation works — potentially for longer than in wheat dough. Below is every raw finding, organized by pillar, with ratios, percentages, temperatures, times, and named sources.

---

## Pillar 1 — Flour blends and binder systems

### The foundational principle: no single GF flour works alone

Multiple serious sources converge on one rule: **a blend of flours plus starches plus a binder is mandatory.** The consensus "sweet spot" ratio is approximately 60% flours (typically rice-based) and 40% starches (tapioca + potato), plus a binder system. Each component plays a distinct role that the synthesis agent must understand.

**White rice flour** provides neutral-flavored structure and crispness, but must be superfine-ground or the crust will taste sandy. **Brown rice flour** adds wheaty, nutty flavor and slightly more protein than white rice; it absorbs more water. **Tapioca starch** is the chew-and-stretch ingredient — multiple sources call it the single most important component for making GF pizza feel like real pizza. It also promotes browning. **Potato starch** (not potato flour — these are different products) retains moisture, adds tender crumb, and contributes to a crispy exterior. **Sorghum flour** provides wheat-like flavor and ~10–11g protein per cup but can make dough dry or tough if overused. **Millet flour** gives buttery color and creamy texture but turns bitter in excess. **Oat flour** provides warm, earthy flavor similar to whole wheat but must be certified GF and some celiacs still react to avenin. **Almond flour** cannot form dough alone but at 10–15% of total adds fat-driven browning, crispness, and protein. **Buckwheat flour** has ~12g protein per cup and distinctive earthy flavor — ATK eliminated it from their blend for being "too distinctive," but it is a key component in Caputo Fioreglut. **Sweet white rice flour (mochiko)** is gummier and stickier than regular rice flour; it's the lead ingredient in Bob's Red Mill 1:1. **Milk powder** dramatically improves Maillard browning and provides proteins that help structure — ATK considers it key to their blend's success.

### The best documented custom blends

**America's Test Kitchen custom blend** (from "How Can It Be Gluten-Free"): ~24 oz white rice flour, 7.5 oz brown rice flour, 7 oz potato starch, 4 oz tapioca starch, 0.75 oz nonfat milk powder. For their pizza specifically, they add **71g almond flour** ("to introduce fat and increase crispiness without leaving the crust greasy") and **4.5 tsp psyllium husk powder** ("crucial to the structure of the crust"). ATK's rationale: "white rice flour for starch, brown rice flour for wheaty flavor, potato starch for tenderness, tapioca starch for spring and stretch, and milk powder for browning and structure."

**CookLikeMom.net blend**: 2 parts brown rice flour : 1 part sorghum : 1 part tapioca starch : 1 part potato starch (by weight: 120g/60g/60g/60g for 300g total). Tip: "For an even better texture, you can lightly toast your rice and sorghum flours in a dry skillet."

**Snixy Kitchen blend**: oat flour + sorghum + millet + brown rice + sweet white rice + tapioca starch + corn starch, bound with psyllium husk. Uses milk instead of water for casein protein structure. Adds apple cider vinegar.

**The Loopy Whisk blend**: millet flour + tapioca starch + sorghum flour. Deliberately keeps starches low to avoid gumminess. Psyllium husk is the sole binder — "YOU CAN'T SUBSTITUTE IT."

### The binder debate: psyllium husk vs. xanthan gum

This is perhaps the most consequential decision in GF pizza formulation. The two dominant binders work through fundamentally different mechanisms and produce different textures.

**Psyllium husk powder** absorbs water to form a gel that ATK describes as creating "a sturdy network capable of holding in lots of gas and steam during baking." An MDPI Foods (2024) study found that at 10% psyllium, SEM images showed a "gluten-like structure" that embedded starch granules and improved gas retention during proofing. Baker's percentages range from **2–5%** across serious recipes: ATK uses ~2–3% in their pizza, Sensible Edibles bakery uses 3%, Gluten Free on a Shoestring suggests ~4.8% as a xanthan replacement. The Gluten Free Alchemist gives a broader range of 2–8.8% depending on application. Psyllium's advantages are bread-like chew, natural ingredient status, fiber content, and what ATK calls adding "wheat flavor that works well in breads." Its critical limitation: the gel **breaks down at approximately 176°F (80°C)**, creating a dangerous "handoff window" during baking. As Annalise Roberts (GF baking authority) explains: "At the point where the psyllium husk starts to fail as a gel, if the bread contains tapioca starch or potato starch, the tapioca starch can act like a bridge until the egg proteins take over to help hold the bread up." This means psyllium works best alongside tapioca starch and/or eggs. Brand matters enormously: ATK found that Yerba Prima and Vitamin Shoppe brands turned bread purple-gray; **Now Foods** worked best. Adding acid (apple cider vinegar, lemon juice) also prevents the purple discoloration.

**Xanthan gum** is a polysaccharide that "strengthens protein networks in GF flours and makes them more elastic" (ATK). It is stable across all baking temperatures — no breakdown window. Baker's percentages for pizza/bread: **0.7–2%** by weight. Nicole Hunn (Gluten Free on a Shoestring) uses 3 tsp per 420g flour (~2.1%). Its advantages are industrial-strength binding at tiny amounts, temperature stability, and proven performance. Its disadvantages: too much creates a gummy, slimy texture; some people report GI sensitivity; it doesn't provide the bread-like chew that psyllium does. The Gluten Free Alchemist states flatly: "Xanthan gum is a wonderful 'all-round' binder… However, it is not the best choice when making gluten-free bread. It simply doesn't have the qualities needed to replicate the texture and structure we crave."

**Combination approaches** are recommended by several professional sources. Sensible Edibles bakery formula: psyllium 3% + xanthan 0.5–1% + ground flax 5% — "This combo gives you bread that's chewy and moist." Vindulge uses Caputo Fioreglut (which has guar + psyllium built in) and adds xanthan separately.

**Other binders documented**: ground flax (5–10% of flour weight, weaker than psyllium or xanthan, adds omega-3s); eggs (provide protein structure that supports rise after psyllium gel weakens — Roberts specifically notes many successful psyllium recipes quietly depend on eggs); guar gum (in Caputo Fioreglut, similar to xanthan but "imparts a slightly starchy texture" per ATK); Expandex modified tapioca starch (Nicole Hunn recommends replacing 36g of AP blend with Expandex for chewier pizza, adding 1.5 oz more water); transglutaminase + sodium caseinate (Modernist Pantry's approach — cross-links milk proteins to create actual gluten-like networks, enabling dough that handles like wheat dough at 110% hydration, but requires specialty ingredients and is not dairy-free).

### Commercial GF flour blends: a ranked assessment

**Caputo Fioreglut — the consensus gold standard for pizza.** Ingredients: GF wheat starch (washed to <5 ppm gluten per batch), dextrose, maize starch, buckwheat flour, rice starch, psyllium seed fiber, guar gum, flavoring. It already contains both psyllium and guar as binders. The dough is actually kneadable and stretchable — rare for GF. Recommended hydration: **80%** per Vindulge (67% was "too dry and stiff"). Endorsements from: Vindulge ("by far the superior dough"), Wheat by the Wayside ("the closest thing I've ever made to a REAL pizza"), Good For You Gluten Free ("life-changing"), Alex Koons (pizza consultant, 17 years), BakingSubs ("the gold standard… genuinely rivals wheat-based pizza"), Nicole Hunn. Downsides: expensive (~$15/2.2lb), contains wheat starch (safe for celiacs per FDA <20 ppm, but NOT safe for wheat allergy), struggles to brown without oil brushing, can be hard to source locally.

**Bob's Red Mill 1-to-1 GF Baking Flour**: sweet white rice flour, brown rice flour, potato starch, sorghum flour, tapioca flour, xanthan gum. Contains xanthan but Nicole Hunn says "not enough to hold yeast bread together" — add 1 extra tsp. Bob's Red Mill's own website says it is "not meant for yeast dough." Best for thicker, pan-style crusts. Optimal hydration: 75% per Vindulge.

**King Arthur Measure for Measure**: rice flour, brown rice flour, sorghum flour, tapioca starch, potato starch, cellulose, xanthan gum, cellulose gum, vitamins. Contains both xanthan AND cellulose gum. King Arthur's own site says it is "best suited for non-yeasted recipes." Fine grind reduces grittiness.

**King Arthur GF Pizza Flour** (boxed): contains GF wheat starch, rice flour, tapioca starch, potato starch, xanthan gum. Alexandra Stafford (Pizza Every Friday Substack) uses this on a baking steel at 550°F convection and calls it revelatory. Hydration: **121%** (more water than flour by weight — dough is batter-like).

**Cup4Cup** (reformulated October 2024): rice flour blend, cornstarch, tapioca flour, potato starch, xanthan gum. Milk powder was removed in reformulation. Originally developed by Chef Lena Kwak with Thomas Keller. Better for delicate pastry than heavy-duty pizza.

### Protein content: a different game in GF

Wheat bread flour has ~12.7% protein from glutenin and gliadin, which form the elastic gluten network. GF flour proteins (from rice, sorghum, chickpea, etc.) **cannot form a gluten network regardless of protein percentage.** ATK states: "Gluten-free flours have less protein than wheat flours and are not capable of forming the same network required to stretch and surround starch granules." The strategy in GF is to recruit protein from multiple non-gluten sources for structure and browning: milk powder provides casein for Maillard reaction and emulsification; almond flour provides fat-accompanied protein for browning; sorghum provides wheat-like body. Protein content per cup (approximate): white rice 6g, brown rice 7–8g, sorghum 10–11g, buckwheat 12g, oat 12g, chickpea 20–21g, almond 21g, tapioca/potato/corn starch ~0g.

## Pillar 2 — Hydration, fermentation, and structure

### Hydration must increase dramatically

Without gluten's elastic protein network holding moisture internally, GF doughs rely on starches and hydrocolloids that require substantially more water to hydrate, swell, and form gels. **The original recipe's 66.7% hydration is too low for any GF flour blend.** Tested hydration ranges from named sources:

Vindulge tested systematically with Caputo Fioreglut: 67% "too dry and stiff — we do not recommend anything less than 70%"; 70% "some elasticity but still slightly dry"; 75% good for NY-style; **80% optimal for Neapolitan-style.** Leopard Crust Pizza (Feng Chen) uses **80%** with Fioreglut. Diego Vitagliano (10Pizzeria, Naples) uses **85%** with pure GF rice flour. ATK uses approximately **124%** — an extremely high hydration that produces batter-like consistency. Alexandra's Kitchen uses **121%** with King Arthur GF Pizza Flour. Nicole Hunn (Fioreglut recipe) uses **~93%** hydration.

The synthesis agent should target **75–85% hydration** for a handleable dough, or higher for batter-style approaches that require par-baking in a pan. Hydration interacts with flour blend: Caputo Fioreglut absorbs far more water than Bob's Red Mill 1:1. Swapping blends without adjusting hydration is a documented failure mode.

What happens at wrong hydration: too low (<70%) produces dry, stiff, crumbly dough with no rise. Too high produces an unfixable "gum line" — that raw-looking wet layer between bottom crust and toppings. Breadtopia specifically warns: "If a gluten free dough is too wet, the crust will look like it is undercooked, but extra baking and even parbaking the crust will NOT fix the gum line."

### Cold fermentation works — and may be more forgiving than wheat

This is one of the most important findings for the synthesis agent. **Cold fermentation develops flavor in GF dough and can work for extended periods.** Feng Chen (Leopard Crust Pizza) reports: "I have used GF dough up to 8 days after starting the cold ferment. No issues, only flavour." He also states GF dough is "almost impossible to overferment" and that even past-peak bulk ferment produced "incredibly tasty GF pizza." His process: room temperature bulk ferment until doubled (~16–24 hours), then dough-balling, then cold ferment 1–2+ days, using 0.6% instant yeast.

The Obsessed Cook uses cold bulk fermentation up to 36 hours for GF sourdough Neapolitan pizza. Nicole Hunn says dough can be stored "in the refrigerator for at least 3 days." GoGlutenFreely's Caputo recipe cold-ferments dough balls 3–24 hours. Gozney (Adam Atkins) recommends "fridge 2–4 days." Alexandra's Kitchen stores shaped dough balls "in the fridge for up to 1 week."

**Contrasting caution:** The Fresh Loaf community warns that "gluten free goes from perfectly proofed to over proofed in a very short amount of time." Another baker there: "GF doughs go from fermented to over fermented very quickly." The reconciliation: these warnings primarily concern **room-temperature** proofing, where GF dough should only rise ~50%, not double. Cold fermentation dramatically slows yeast activity and makes extended ferments safe. The specific flour blend also matters — Caputo Fioreglut is purpose-designed for extended fermentation. **A 48–72 hour cold ferment analogous to the original wheat recipe appears viable, particularly with Caputo Fioreglut and low yeast amounts.**

Yeast itself doesn't care whether flour has gluten. The Obsessed Cook notes that during autolyse, "starch-digesting enzymes (amylase) start converting starch into sugars — these sugars will be available for yeast to devour." Yeast activity is identical; the difference is that without gluten's gas-trapping ability, CO2 produced during fermentation is more easily lost. This is why the Obsessed Cook emphasizes: "Considering gluten-free flours retain less gases than gluten-containing flours, I refrain from degassing the dough too much."

### Diastatic malt powder must be replaced

**Standard diastatic malt powder is NOT gluten-free.** It is made from malted barley flour, often blended with wheat flour. King Arthur Baking confirms this explicitly. Modern Mountain Baking adds: "Since the active enzymes in diastatic malt powder specifically work on gluten development, a gluten-free version would not provide the same benefits."

GF alternatives with enzymatic activity: **Sprouted rice flour or buckwheat flour** has low-level diastatic activity. A 2025 MDPI Molecules study produced GF rice malt extract powder "rich in fermentable sugars (maltose 43.9 g/100g, glucose 14.3 g/100g)" that supported yeast growth comparably to commercial malt. Malted millet has "strong enough diastatic activity to convert its own starches, but that's about it" per a homebrewer. **Pure amylase powder** is available and more potent per BlogChef. **Koji (Aspergillus oryzae)** offers strong enzymatic activity. Weekend Bakery notes: "GF [malt] powders have way less 'power' (enzymatic activity) than the regular version."

GF alternatives for browning and flavor without enzymatic action: honey (1–2%, participates in Maillard reaction via its reducing sugars), sugar, rice malt syrup, and especially **milk powder** (extra lactose drives browning — King Arthur notes this specifically). For the synthesis agent: the original recipe's 1% diastatic malt served two purposes — enzymatic starch-to-sugar conversion during fermentation and Maillard browning. In GF, these functions may need to be addressed separately: honey or sugar for yeast food, milk powder or oil-brushing for browning.

### Oven spring and the puffy cornicione are achievable

Peter Bronski (No Gluten, No Problem) calls a GF Neapolitan cornicione "one of the Holy Grails of gluten-free pizza." Diego Vitagliano (10Pizzeria, Naples) achieves it with pure rice flour at 85% hydration — La Cucina Italiana reports you "won't long for the traditional one." The Loopy Whisk achieves "a puffy, chewy crust with a gorgeous open crumb and a thin base" using psyllium husk.

The gas-expansion mechanism is fundamentally similar: yeast CO2 and steam drive oven spring. But the gas-trapping system is different. In wheat dough, the elastic gluten network stretches to accommodate expanding gas. In GF dough, this role is filled by: (1) the hydrocolloid gel matrix (psyllium/xanthan), which provides viscosity and traps gas; (2) swollen starch granules embedded in the gel; and (3) the critical timing of starch gelatinization — if starch sets too early, it constrains expansion. MDPI Polymers (2022) found that hydrocolloids delay gelatinization by competing with starch for water, giving more time for volume expansion. Higher gelatinization temperature = higher final bread volume.

Techniques to maximize GF oven spring: brush the cornicione with water before baking (King Arthur, No Gluten No Problem); brush crust with olive oil to prevent drying; use psyllium husk as primary binder; maintain high baking temperature on a well-preheated steel; minimize degassing during shaping; consider par-baking without toppings so the crust can puff freely. Nicole Hunn: "The rise of the pizza crust itself will be mostly in the oven during the first bake, before you add toppings."

### The science of GF dough structure

Academic literature (Lazaridou et al., J Food Engineering 2007) studied hydrocolloids in rice flour/corn starch GF formulations and found "xanthan had the most pronounced effect on viscoelastic properties yielding strengthened doughs; addition of xanthan to the gluten-free formulation resulted in a farinograph curve typical of wheat flour doughs." MDPI Polymers (2022) confirmed: "By adding comparatively small amounts of hydrocolloids to starch, one can achieve similar thermo-mechanical effects by the addition of gluten."

GF dough progresses through distinct phases (Román et al., ScienceDirect): **mixing** creates a "liquid foam" — air is incorporated but held by viscous batter, not elastic dough; **proofing** inflates this foam via yeast CO2; **baking** transitions the liquid foam to a "solid foam" (crust, where water evaporates at >170°C) and a "foamed gel" (crumb, where starch gelatinizes); **cooling** sets the final structure via starch retrogradation.

Starch retrogradation — the recrystallization of amylopectin chains — is more pronounced in GF products because there is no gluten network to buffer moisture distribution. This means GF pizza stales faster. Hydrocolloids retard retrogradation: MDPI Foods (2021) found that hydrogen bonding between hydrocolloids and starch slows staling. Psyllium specifically "decreased crumb firmness values by 65–75% compared to control gluten-free bread during 72h of storage" (PMC 2021). Fat/oil additions also retard staling, which is relevant since many GF recipes include olive oil.

## Pillar 3 — Handling, shaping, and bake method

### GF dough cannot be hand-stretched or tossed

The dough ranges from thick batter to soft, sticky playdough depending on formulation. The most common shaping method across all serious sources is **pressing/spreading with oiled or wet hands on parchment paper.** Gluten Free Palate: "Using oiled hands, spread the dough into a circle about 10 to 12 inches in diameter on a piece of parchment paper." Minimalist Baker: "Work from the middle and push to spread the dough, aiming for less than ¼ inch thick."

Alternative methods: **rolling with a rolling pin** between parchment sheets (Eat With Clarity, Vindulge, Alexandra's Kitchen); **pressing into an oiled pan** such as cast iron (King Arthur, A Hint of Rosemary); **finger-pressing on a floured surface** then transferring (The Loopy Whisk — closest to traditional, but only works with psyllium-bound, lower-hydration doughs). No sources recommended piping from a bag or using a tortilla press. Cold dough is universally easier to handle — Nicole Hunn recommends shaping straight from the refrigerator.

### The baking steel + broiler method works, with modifications

Multiple sources confirm that a preheated baking steel works for GF pizza and is in fact strongly recommended. Let Them Eat GF Cake: "I've found a pizza steel to be the best option… They conduct heat 20 times better than a stone." The key modifications:

**Parchment paper is nearly mandatory** for launching GF dough onto a preheated steel. The dough is too soft and wet to slide directly off a peel in most formulations. Baking Steel's own GF recipe uses parchment. Alexandra's Kitchen: "Shimmy the dough, parchment paper and all, onto the preheated Steel." One exception: FoodSocial/Primal Palate launches directly using stone-ground GF flour as "ball bearings" under the dough — but this requires a very firm dough. For safety, trim parchment flush with the dough to prevent charring at temperatures above 500°F.

**The broiler finish works.** Let Them Eat GF Cake uses the steel + broiler method explicitly: "Bake first at 500°F for 6–7 minutes. Then switch to broil to melt the toppings until bubbly." Balsam Kitchen: "Optional: Finish under broil for 1–2 minutes if you like charred edges. Watch carefully during broiling to prevent burning." Flour Farm: "Turn the oven temperature to Low Broil… put the baking sheet or skillets back in the oven for 2+ minutes." True leopard spotting requires 800°F+ (pizza oven territory), but the broiler provides a reasonable approximation. Leopard Crust Pizza confirmed that GF pizza CAN get spotty char using Caputo Fioreglut at 750°F in a pizza oven.

### Par-baking is nearly universal and likely essential

Par-baking the crust before adding toppings is the single most common technique across all serious GF pizza sources. Eatmuch.blog: "Parbaking is not optional when working with gluten-free dough… This crucial step allows the starches in the dough to set their structure before they encounter any moisture from toppings." Gluten Free Palate: "Skipping the par-bake step is the most common cause of soggy pizza."

**Par-baking times and temperatures vary with oven heat:**

At high heat (500–550°F) on steel: **2–7 minutes** par-bake. Baking Steel official: 6–7 min par-bake + 3–5 min with toppings. Alexandra's Kitchen at 550°F convection: 2 min par-bake + 4–5 min topped. FoodSocial at 550°F: 2 min par-bake + 10–12 min topped. Balsam Kitchen at 550°F: 6–8 min par-bake + 6 min topped + 1–2 min broiler.

At moderate heat (400–425°F): **8–20 minutes** par-bake. Meaningful Eats at 425°F: 15–20 min par-bake on pan, then transfer to steel for 10–15 min with toppings. Mama Knows GF at 425°F: 15 min par-bake + 10–15 min topped.

ATK uses a unique cold-start approach: place in cold oven, set to 325°F, bake 45–50 min (low and slow to drive off excess moisture), then top and bake on preheated stone at 500°F for 10–12 min.

**Notable exception:** Vindulge (using Caputo Fioreglut on pizza stone) says "there is no need to par-bake this dough" if you have a pizza stone and correct hydration.

### Temperature and timing: GF takes roughly twice as long

GF pizza generally requires **longer total bake time** than wheat pizza at the same temperature. Eat Healthy 365: "Generally, a gluten-free pizza bakes at 450–500°F for about 10 to 15 minutes" total, versus 6–10 minutes for wheat. Breadtopia: "About 13 minutes on a hot stone vs. 8 minutes for wheat." This means the synthesis agent should plan for 10–15 minutes total (par-bake + topped) at 500°F on steel, not the 5–7 minutes the original wheat recipe might use.

GF dough browns differently and generally needs help. The Loopy Whisk: "Gluten free bread in general has a greater tendency to dry out, especially when you bake it at a very high heat… it has a tendency to form a dull, dry, white-ish crust." Solutions: brush exposed crust with olive oil before baking; add milk powder to the flour blend; use honey or sugar in the dough; brush with egg wash. Internal temperature when done: **200°F** per multiple sources.

### Achieving a crispy bottom without a dry interior

This requires attacking the problem from multiple angles simultaneously. A preheated steel is essential — every serious source recommends 30–60 minutes of preheating. Oil on the dough and/or in the pan promotes browning and crispness. The two-stage bake (par-bake on parchment/pan, then finish on bare steel) is Meaningful Eats' recommended approach for maximum bottom crispness. Don't over-top: GF crust handles less weight than wheat. Tasty at Home: "Stick to ¾ cup sauce and 1 cup cheese for a 12-inch pizza." Pre-cook or pat dry high-moisture toppings. Let the finished pizza rest 3–5 minutes before slicing to let the starch structure set.

Hydration management is critical: too-wet dough creates a gum line that no amount of baking will fix (Breadtopia). Dish Bloom: "Most gluten-free failures trace back to low heat or low hydration — solve those, and the dough performs."

## Pillar 4 — Best-in-class recipes and sources

### The technically rigorous benchmark: America's Test Kitchen

ATK's GF pizza (from "How Can It Be Gluten-Free") is the most scientifically developed recipe found. Flour: 16 oz ATK custom blend + 71g almond flour. Binder: 4.5 tsp psyllium husk powder. Leavening: 1 tsp instant yeast + 2.5 tsp baking powder (dual leavening for maximum openness). Hydration: ~124% (2.5 cups warm water + ¼ cup vegetable oil per 16 oz flour — batter-like). Method: stand mixer with paddle, 6 min; rise ~1.5 hours; shape on parchment into 11.5-inch round; **cold-start oven at 325°F for 45–50 minutes** to drive off moisture; then top and bake on preheated stone at 500°F for 10–12 minutes. The cold-start par-bake is ATK's unique innovation — it cooks the interior without burning the exterior. Many celiac families report making 15+ crusts at a time to freeze.

### The neo-Neapolitan on steel: Alexandra Stafford (Pizza Every Friday)

**This is the closest published match to the target use case.** Flour: 233g King Arthur GF Pizza Flour. Hydration: **121%** (284g water). Yeast: 2 tsp instant. Rise: 2–3 hours at room temp; can store shaped balls in fridge up to 1 week. Baking: **steel in top third of oven, preheat to 550°F convection roast for 1 hour.** Roll dough thin on floured surface. Par-bake on steel until puffed. Top. Return to oven 4–5 min until blistered. Key quote: "I use more salt, and rather than shape my rounds Neapolitan-style with a puffy outer rim, I roll them thinly and parbake them, which produces a pizza with a thin and crisp crust."

### The Caputo Fioreglut standard: Vindulge

Sean Martin (Vindulge, author of "Fire + Wine Backyard Pizza") strongly recommends Caputo Fioreglut at **80% hydration**. Adds xanthan gum and baking powder to supplement the flour's built-in psyllium + guar. Uses fresh active yeast (not instant — "creates too much rise"). No par-baking needed with a pizza stone. Can freeze dough. Rated 4.73/5. Says 80% hydration is "wet enough for good rise/bubbles but workable."

### Nicole Hunn's two approaches (Gluten Free on a Shoestring)

**Classic recipe**: 420g GP flour blend, 3 tsp xanthan gum, instant yeast. Beat until dough "looks whipped." Rise 1 hour or cold-ferment up to 3 days. Par-bake 5 min, top, finish 5–7 min. Over 900 five-star reviews — a celiac community staple since 2011.

**Fioreglut recipe**: 158g Caputo Fioreglut, 147g water (~93% hydration), 1 Tbsp EVOO, 1 tsp instant yeast, ¾ tsp sugar, ½ tsp salt. Mix 3 min. Rise ~1 hour at 70°F. Press into 14-inch round with oiled fingers. Par-bake ~5 min. Creates pizza that "bends and folds." Rated 4.85/5.

### Feng Chen (Leopard Crust Pizza): the long-ferment champion

Uses Caputo Fioreglut at **80% hydration** (320g flour, 256g water), 5% olive oil, 3% salt, 0.6% instant yeast. Room temperature bulk ferment until doubled (~16–24 hours), then dough-balling, then cold ferment 1–2+ days. Reports using dough up to **8 days** after starting cold ferment with "no issues, only flavour." Achieves spotty char/leopard spots at 750°F in pizza oven. States GF dough is "almost impossible to overferment."

### Diego Vitagliano (10Pizzeria, Naples): pure rice flour Neapolitan

Uses **pure GF rice flour** at **85% hydration** (1kg flour, 850g water, 15g salt, 3g yeast). Achieves a cornicione so impressive that La Cucina Italiana says "you won't long for the traditional one." This is the most minimalist acclaimed approach found.

### Meaningful Eats: the two-stage steel method

GF 1:1 baking flour + ¼ cup almond flour + psyllium husk powder. Dual leavening (yeast + baking powder). Par-bake on pan at 425°F for 15–20 minutes, then slide onto pizza steel for 10–15 min with toppings. This two-stage approach (pan → steel) is directly relevant to the steel-under-broiler target.

### The Loopy Whisk: psyllium-only, finger-shaped

Custom blend of millet + tapioca + sorghum with psyllium husk as sole binder. Very high hydration producing soft, slack dough. Makes psyllium gel first, then combines with dry ingredients. Shaped by finger-pressing on floured surface — closest to traditional hand-shaping. Baked on preheated steel at 475°F for 15–18 min total without par-baking.

### Modernist Pantry: transglutaminase approach

Uses transglutaminase ("meat glue") + sodium caseinate + GF flour at **110% hydration**. Dough transforms from liquid batter to firm dough during a 45-minute rest as transglutaminase cross-links casein proteins. Rolled out "beautifully" and baked "light and crispy." The most modernist approach found — produces dough that genuinely handles like wheat dough.

### Professional pizzerias

Italy's high celiac diagnosis rate has produced a strong GF pizza culture. Caputo developed Fioreglut specifically for this market and recommends baking at **698°F (370°C)**. Pizzeria Libretto (Toronto) previously had a dedicated GF oven and uses 72-hour sourdough fermentation for regular crust. GF crust recipe is proprietary. Little Nonna (Paris) operates as a dedicated 100% GF Italian restaurant.

## Pillar 5 — Pitfalls and non-obvious knowledge

### The mistakes that ruin everything

**Adding flour when dough feels sticky.** This is the wheat baker's instinct that destroys GF dough. Gluten Free Palate: "Do not add more flour to compensate. Instead, oil your hands and any utensils generously." GF dough is supposed to be sticky. It firms up as starches and binders hydrate over 10–15 minutes.

**Using regular-grind rice flour instead of superfine.** Nicole Hunn is emphatic: "A blender or a food processor, no matter how powerful, will not make rice flour that is finely ground. Those appliances will only make gritty rice flour, which is precisely what makes gluten free baked goods subpar." Authentic Foods Superfine is the gold standard. Bob's Red Mill is "fine but not quite superfine." Ranking per Annalise Roberts: Authentic Foods > Bob's Red Mill = King Arthur > Arrowhead Mills (gritty).

**Letting GF dough double in size.** It should rise only ~50%. Breadtopia: "Letting the dough rise more results in a bread structure that collapses once it is baked." Over-proofing is fast at room temperature — GF dough lacks gluten's structural resistance.

**Skipping the par-bake.** The single most cited cause of soggy GF pizza. The starches need to set before encountering moisture from toppings.

**Using expired or dead yeast.** Gluten Free Palate calls this "the number one reason gluten-free pizza dough fails." Always proof active dry yeast; use instant yeast for reliability.

**Over-topping.** Universal warning. GF crust is structurally weaker. Maximum ~1 cup cheese for a 12-inch pie.

**Not weighing ingredients.** Volume measurements are wildly inaccurate for GF flour blends. Nearly every serious source insists on a scale.

### What looks optional but matters enormously

**Rest time after mixing and before shaping.** GF flours and especially psyllium need time to fully hydrate. Cathy's Gluten Free: dough "will firm up while it sits as the flours and psyllium husk absorb moisture." Ten to fifteen minutes minimum.

**Apple cider vinegar.** Appears in the majority of serious GF pizza recipes for three distinct reasons: strengthens dough structure and stabilizes air bubbles (The Banana Diaries); improves yeast activity by slightly increasing acidity (Gluten Free Alchemist: "The acidic cider vinegar helps to increase the bubbling of the yeast for best texture"); prevents psyllium husk from turning dough purple (Planted365).

**Order of ingredient addition.** Psyllium should be pre-hydrated into a gel before combining with dry ingredients (The Loopy Whisk, Planted365). When using xanthan, whisk dry ingredients first, add liquids all at once, and beat for 5 minutes to develop the xanthan network.

**Milk proteins substitute for gluten's structural role.** Snixy Kitchen: "The proteins in milk help provide structure to the dough by mimicking gluten. The outer crust won't be quite as fluffy without it."

**Expandex modified tapioca starch** improves moisture retention, chewiness, elasticity, and extends shelf life. Nicole Hunn offers it as an "optional variation for chewier pizza." Baking Business found GF bread with Expandex "had enhanced flavor and improved moistness when microwaved and showed improved crispiness and chewiness when toasted."

**Tangzhong may be redundant in GF baking.** The Fresh Loaf: "You really don't need tangzhong in GF baking because you already are creating that starchy gel by the nature of the ingredients." However, Sunflour GF Academy disagrees, calling it "a game-changer."

### Storage behavior

GF dough can be refrigerated and generally improves for 1–3 days (flavor development from slow fermentation). Most recipes cap cold storage at **24 hours**, though Nicole Hunn says "up to 3 days" and Feng Chen reports up to 8 days. Raw dough and par-baked crusts both freeze well for up to 3 months; par-baked crusts are the preferred make-ahead strategy across nearly all sources.

**GF pizza stales faster than wheat pizza.** The lack of gluten network causes faster moisture migration from crumb to crust, accelerating starch retrogradation. Critically, **refrigeration (4°C) accelerates starch retrogradation more than room temperature or freezing** — so leftover GF pizza in the fridge stales faster than wheat pizza would. Freezing at -18°C essentially halts new crystal formation, making it the superior preservation method.

### Reheating

Avoid microwave alone — it makes GF crust rubbery. Best methods: hot skillet on stovetop with a lid (to re-crisp bottom and melt cheese), or oven reheating at 375°F for 5–7 minutes on a preheated surface. Expandex-enhanced GF bread "showed improved crispiness and chewiness when toasted" per Baking Business.

### Multi-pie session management

GF dough is more time-sensitive at room temperature than wheat. Keep unused dough balls **refrigerated and covered** — pull each one 20–30 minutes before shaping. Vindulge: "If you don't [cover them] then the outer portion of the dough will develop a hard crust that is unpleasant to form." The baking steel needs 5–10 minutes to recover heat between pies (steel recovers faster than stone). Shape the next pizza while the previous one bakes and the steel reheats. Total per-pizza time including par-bake: 10–15 minutes at 500°F, roughly double the wheat workflow.

### Counterintuitive findings

GF dough should be beaten vigorously in a mixer (to develop the binder network) but not kneaded by hand (there's no gluten to develop). The dough should feel too sticky — this is correct. GF dough behaves more like **high-hydration rye** than wheat: think batter bread, not elastic dough. Psyllium husk creates a gel that breaks down at ~176°F, after which tapioca starch and/or egg proteins must take over structural duties — this "handoff window" is invisible but critical. A gum line from excess moisture cannot be fixed by longer baking; it's a formulation error. The broiler method from the original recipe translates well to GF — GF dough can get charred and blistered — but needs more total time and benefits from an olive oil brush on exposed crust to prevent the characteristic GF white, dull appearance.

### Cross-contamination for celiac households

A shared baking steel is safe if properly cleaned (stainless steel is non-porous), but parchment paper between dough and steel provides an extra barrier. Vincentini et al. (2016, Journal of Food Protection) found that "simultaneous cooking of gluten-containing and gluten-free pizzas in shared ovens has demonstrated a low risk of contamination" when staff were trained — all tested below 20 ppm. Convection ovens carry slightly higher risk because fans can blow wheat particles. The biggest home risk is airborne wheat flour dust settling on GF prep surfaces — clean thoroughly before GF prep. Wooden cutting boards and rolling pins are porous and should not be shared.

| Original wheat element | GF translation | Status |
|---|---|---|
| 66.7% hydration | **75–85%** (Fioreglut) or 100–124% (batter-style) | Must increase substantially |
| 2.4% salt | Similar percentage likely works | Survives |
| 1% diastatic malt powder (barley) | Replace with 1–2% honey/sugar + milk powder in blend for browning | Must change — barley malt is not GF |
| 2% olive oil | Likely increase to 3–5%; also brush on exposed crust | Survives but increase |
| 0.11% instant dry yeast | Similar or slightly higher; 0.3–0.6% for shorter ferments | Survives with possible adjustment |
| 72-hour cold ferment | **Works** — possibly up to 8 days with Fioreglut | Survives |
| Hand-stretch and launch onto steel | Press/roll on parchment; launch parchment onto steel | Must change |
| Single bake on steel under broiler | Par-bake 2–7 min, top, finish 4–8 min, broil 1–2 min | Must add par-bake step |
