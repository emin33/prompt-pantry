# Spicy Pork Belly Yakiniku: A Comprehensive Culinary Research Compendium

**The ideal spicy yakiniku pork belly lives at the crossroads of two traditions**: Japanese yakiniku tare (soy-mirin-sesame-fruit) and Korean dwaeji bulgogi (gochujang-gochugaru-garlic-ginger-pear). No single pure tradition delivers the sweet-spicy-savory trifecta — the target flavor profile emerges from their fusion. Across dozens of acclaimed sources, a clear consensus emerges: **soy sauce, gochujang, sesame oil, garlic, ginger, mirin, and grated Asian pear form the essential core**, with dual-chili layering (gochujang paste + gochugaru flakes) separating expert-level recipes from ordinary ones. The Osaka/Tsuruhashi yakiniku corridor — where Korean and Japanese flavors have cross-pollinated for 80 years — is the geographic and cultural epicenter where this fusion occurs most authentically.

## 1. Renowned recipes and where they converge

### The top-tier sources

**Korean Bapsang's Jeyuk Bokkeum** (Hyosun Ro) carries the highest user validation at **4.85/5 from 206 votes** and represents the definitive Korean spicy pork. Its marinade anchors on a generous 6 tablespoons of gochujang per 2 pounds of meat, layered with gochugaru, soy, mirin, sugar, honey, sesame oil, and — critically — half a grated apple for enzymatic tenderization. Hyosun emphasizes that "lots of fresh garlic and ginger are very important to this recipe" and that ginger specifically reduces pork's gamey quality.

**Maangchi's Dwaeji Bulgogi** is the single most cross-referenced Korean spicy pork recipe on the internet. Her canonical version centers on ½ cup crushed Korean pear per pound of pork belly (sliced ⅛-inch thick), with gochujang, soy, rice syrup, sesame oil, garlic, and ginger. The Korean pear is her non-negotiable — it serves dual duty as tenderizer and sweetener.

**Just One Cookbook's BBQ Pork Belly** (Namiko Hirasawa Chen) is the most authoritative English-language source for the Japanese yakiniku side. Her approach is savory-forward: soy sauce, sesame oil, mirin, honey, garlic, ginger, and grated onion as tenderizer, with red chili as optional. She specifically recommends **¼-inch thickness** for outdoor grilling, noting that the traditional ⅛-inch yakiniku slices "burn easily on the grill" with marinated preparations.

**The Junghyun Park/Jungyoon Choi Korean Cookbook recipe**, adapted by Mitchell Davis (former VP of the James Beard Foundation), stands out for its professionally calibrated restraint: 3 tablespoons gochujang, 1 tablespoon each of gochugaru, light soy, mirin, rice syrup, and 1½ teaspoons sesame oil, thinned with 2 tablespoons water. This represents the most precisely measured professional version.

**Food.com's Japanese-Style Pork Yakiniku** provides the purest Japanese approach, uniquely incorporating **miso** (red or white), rice vinegar, and dry mustard alongside the standard soy-mirin-sugar-sesame-garlic base. Its hallmark is an equal-ratio philosophy: 1½ tablespoons each of miso, soy, mirin, sugar, and sesame oil per ½ pound.

**Momofuku's Spicy Pork Belly** brings the professional restaurant perspective, notably finishing with a **Kewpie mayonnaise + reserved marinade dipping sauce** — a clever emulsion that adds richness and cling.

### Consensus across top recipes

The universal core ingredients appearing in 80–100% of acclaimed recipes per pound of meat: **soy sauce** (1–3 Tbsp), **garlic** (2–3 cloves minced), **sesame oil** (1–2 tsp), **a sweetener** (1–2 Tbsp sugar, honey, or rice syrup), **ginger** (1 tsp grated), and **gochujang** (1–3 Tbsp for the spicy versions). Common but not universal additions include gochugaru flakes (adds smoky-fruity heat beyond what gochujang provides), grated Asian pear or apple (enzymatic tenderizer + sweetness), grated onion (tenderizer + flavor base), mirin (subtle sweetness + gloss), and black pepper.

**What separates the best from average**: the dual-chili approach (gochujang for fermented depth AND gochugaru for clean, fruity heat), enzymatic fruit tenderizers (pear or apple), grated onion in the marinade, and the inclusion of a professional umami booster — whether miso, fish sauce, dashi, or MSG.

### Key takeaways

The three most frequently referenced and highest-quality recipes are Maangchi's Dwaeji Bulgogi (widest reach), Korean Bapsang's Jeyuk Bokkeum (highest rated), and Just One Cookbook's BBQ Pork Belly (best yakiniku bridge). The Mitchell Davis/Junghyun Park version offers the most professionally calibrated ratios. The Food.com yakiniku recipe is the best pure-Japanese reference point, uniquely incorporating miso. For the sweet-spicy-savory target, a hybrid drawing from both Korean and Japanese traditions yields the strongest result.

## 2. Technique deep-dive: from prep to plate

### Slicing determines everything downstream

**The consensus ideal thickness is ¼ inch (6 mm) for home grilling** with a marinated preparation. Traditional yakiniku restaurants use ⅛ inch (3 mm) on tabletop grills, but multiple authorities — most notably Just One Cookbook — explicitly warn that ultra-thin slices "burn easily on the grill" when coated in sugar-containing marinades. For the richest char without scorching, ¼ inch gives the right margin of error. The meat should be cut **against the grain**, approximately 2.5–3 inches long by 1.25–1.5 inches wide.

**The par-freeze technique** is consensus best practice for home slicing: wrap the pork belly in plastic, place on a metal tray (conducts cold faster), and freeze for 1.5–2 hours until firm but not rock-solid. The meat should yield slightly under pressure but hold rigid enough for clean, even cuts. A sharp gyuto or chef's knife with a gentle sawing motion produces the best results. The far easier path: buy pre-sliced pork belly at an Asian grocery store (H-Mart, etc.), where commercial meat slicers produce perfectly uniform cuts. Always use **skin-off** — pork skin doesn't render at the temperatures and brief cook times involved in yakiniku and becomes tough and chewy.

Professional yakiniku chefs also employ **kakushi-bocho (hidden scoring)** — shallow diagonal cuts across the surface that sever muscle fibers to prevent curling, improve tenderness, and allow marinade to penetrate deeper.

### Marination science favors thin cuts

A critical insight from food science research (Meathead/AmazingRibs citing Prof. Greg Blonder, Kenji López-Alt, Tim Chin at Serious Eats): **marinades penetrate rarely more than ⅛ inch (3 mm)** into meat, even after hours of soaking. Only salt penetrates significantly deeper — about 1 inch in 24 hours. This means thin-sliced pork belly is the *ideal* use case for marinades — at ¼ inch thick, the marinade can effectively reach the center from both sides. For thick cuts, marinades are merely surface treatments.

Each marinade component works through a different mechanism. **Salt/soy sauce** is the most important — sodium chloride's tiny molecular size allows it to penetrate deepest, dissolving the muscle protein myosin, loosening fibers, and improving water retention. **Sugar** does not penetrate deeply but fuels Maillard browning and caramelization at the surface. **Enzymatic tenderizers** (Asian pear, apple, kiwi, ginger, grated onion) contain cysteine proteases that hydrolyze peptide bonds, with pear being the gentlest and safest for longer marinades and kiwi being 60% more potent than pineapple (per NIH research) — so powerful it can turn thin slices mushy in under 30 minutes. **Acids** denature surface proteins but risk creating a mushy exterior if over-applied on thin cuts.

**Optimal marination time**: 30 minutes minimum for surface flavor; **4–12 hours is the consensus sweet spot** for ¼-inch slices; overnight is acceptable for mild enzymatic marinades (pear/apple/onion-based). Beyond 12 hours, mushiness risk rises. With aggressive enzymes like kiwi or fresh pineapple, limit exposure to 30 minutes maximum. Chef Ji Hye Kim (Miss Kim, Ann Arbor) recommends no more than 1–1.5 cups of marinade per pound and "wouldn't marinate longer than an hour" for very thin slices.

### Grilling: managing the caramelization-to-burn window

**Target grill temperature: 450°F (230°C)** — hot enough for Maillard browning (begins at 280–330°F) and caramelization (fructose at 230°F, sucrose at 320°F) but below the pyrolysis threshold (355°F+) where bitter, burnt compounds form. The narrow window between caramelization and burning is the central technical challenge of grilling sugar-laden marinades.

**The two-zone method is the consensus best technique**: sear over direct high heat for approximately 1 minute per side until caramelized edges develop, then move to the indirect/low-heat zone to finish gently. This prevents the sugary marinade from crossing into burning territory. Shake or pat off excess marinade before grilling — pooled liquid means pooled sugar, which means burning.

**Charcoal is the preferred heat source** for its dry radiant heat (promotes Maillard reaction), smoke flavor, and fat-drip drainage. Binchotan (Japanese white charcoal) is the traditional yakiniku fuel — burns long, produces minimal smoke, and doesn't taint flavor. Gas works but produces moisture from combustion that inhibits browning. Cast iron delivers excellent crust from contact but causes pork belly fat to pool rather than drain, producing a crispier, more bacon-like result. Just One Cookbook specifically recommends outdoor grilling for pork belly "so that the extra fat will melt off and the finished product won't be as oily."

**Flip technique**: Just One Cookbook's method is "70% on the front side, 30% on the back" — two flips maximum. Watch for juice beading on the surface as the flip signal. For ¼-inch slices, total cook time is **2–3 minutes**. For ⅛-inch, 30 seconds per side.

### Finishing and serving: eat immediately

**Thin-sliced yakiniku meat should not rest.** The entire yakiniku tradition is built on eating pieces directly off the grill while hot. The physics support this — at such thinness, there's negligible internal temperature gradient to equalize, and the meat's high surface-area-to-volume ratio means it cools rapidly.

Traditional garnishes include toasted white sesame seeds, sliced scallions, and fresh chili. Serve with steamed rice, lettuce wraps (sangchu), ssamjang dipping paste, kimchi, pickled vegetables, and lemon or sudachi wedges. Momofuku's trick of mixing reserved marinade with Kewpie mayo for a dipping sauce is a noteworthy professional touch.

### Key takeaways

The biggest technique debates: pre-marinade (Korean approach) vs. post-grill tare dip (traditional yakiniku) — for the spicy target, pre-marinade wins; sugar in the marinade vs. as a finishing glaze — use moderate sugar in the marinade but control heat carefully; single-flip vs. multiple — for thin marinated pork belly, the two-flip method (70/30) prevents disturbing the developing crust. The most impactful science-backed insight is that **thin slicing is what makes marinades actually work** — it transforms a surface treatment into full-penetration seasoning.

### Secret weapons the pros use

**Miso** appears in only ~20% of recipes but is flagged by multiple professional sources as a major umami amplifier. MasterClass's yakiniku sauce guide recommends adding miso or shio koji "for a sweet boost of umami." The Food.com Japanese yakiniku recipe is the clearest example — equal parts miso, soy, mirin, and sugar create a deeply savory base that is distinctly more Japanese than any gochujang-forward recipe. White miso is milder; red miso is saltier and more assertive. **For the yakiniku-Korean fusion target, adding 1 tablespoon of white miso to a gochujang-based marinade bridges both traditions.**

**Fish sauce** (1–2 tsp) provides a background layer of fermented umami without being detectably fishy. Common in Korean cooking (used in kimchi), it amplifies savory depth through glutamate compounds distinct from soy sauce.

**MSG** is the ingredient every restaurant uses and almost no recipe writer mentions. At ¼ teaspoon per pound, it amplifies umami without adding other flavors. Naturally present in soy sauce, miso, fish sauce, and gochujang — adding a pinch simply concentrates what's already there. Ajinomoto is the standard brand.

**Dashi stock** (kombu + bonito) appears in Seonkyoung Longest's yakiniku sauce recipe, stirred in after simmering the aromatics. It provides the distinctly Japanese umami backbone that separates yakiniku tare from Korean marinades.

**Katakuriko (potato starch)** — borrowed from Chinese velveting technique — creates a protective coating that helps retain moisture during high-heat grilling, noted on Cookpad as making the result "more tender."

**Kewpie mayonnaise mixed with reserved marinade** (Momofuku's approach) creates an emulsified dipping sauce that is richer and clingier than straight tare.

### The sweetener decision matters

Dark brown sugar is the most recommended sweetener across sources for its molasses depth and reliable caramelization. Honey creates the best glaze but burns fastest — apply in the final minutes or use at lower heat. Mirin provides "sweetness and gloss" but isn't sweet enough alone — pair with sugar or honey. **Korean rice syrup (mulyeot)** is the traditional Korean choice, creating a tacky, professional-looking glaze. The key warning from Chef Ji Hye Kim: gochujang already contains sugars from rice fermentation, so additional sweetener must be calibrated carefully — the combined sugar load is what causes burning.

### Key takeaways

The highest-impact ingredients that separate expert recipes from basic ones are grated Asian pear or apple (enzymatic tenderization + natural sweetness), the dual-chili approach (gochujang + gochugaru), grated onion as a tenderizer, and a professional umami booster (miso, fish sauce, dashi, or MSG). For the yakiniku-Korean fusion target, adding miso to a gochujang base creates the most distinctive bridge between traditions. Toasted sesame oil should be added late in marination or as a finishing drizzle — its smoke point (320–350°F) means it burns and turns acrid on a 450°F+ grill.

## 4. Regional and stylistic variations that define the landscape

### Traditional yakiniku: three seasoning philosophies

Modern yakiniku traces to post-WWII Korean immigrants in Osaka and Tokyo who opened "horumon-yaki" (grilled offal) stalls. The term "yakiniku" became a politically neutral compromise in the 1960s when Korean restaurants were divided between North- and South-aligned naming conventions. According to *Nippon Yakiniku Monogatari* by Toshio Miyatsuka, yakiniku was born as a **momi-dare (marinade) culture** — post-war offal had strong odors, so chefs marinated it in garlic, ginger, green onions, soy sauce, and miso.

Three seasoning pillars evolved. **Momi-dare** (pre-marinade) is the original technique — soy, garlic, ginger, miso, and green onion — the ancestor of what we're researching. **Tsuke-dare** (post-grill dipping sauce) became the dominant Japanese home style after commercial yakiniku sauces appeared in the 1960s (Ebara Foods launched the first in 1968): soy, sake, mirin, sugar, garlic, fruit juice, sesame. **Shio** (salt) rose in the 1980s alongside the premium wagyu boom, when better refrigeration eliminated the need for masking odors.

**Pork belly in the yakiniku canon sits below beef in hierarchy** but is a standard menu item. At Gyu-Kaku, it's offered with shio (salt) or sweet soy tare — **not typically spicy in the traditional Japanese framework**. Spice is a Korean-influenced addition. This is why the target flavor profile is inherently a fusion concept.

### Korean dwaeji bulgogi: the spicy pork tradition

Dwaeji bulgogi (돼지불고기, "pork fire-meat") is the definitive Korean spicy grilled pork and **the closest single-tradition match to the target flavor profile**. The marinade IS the dish — gochujang is the star, supported by gochugaru, soy, sesame oil, sugar, generous garlic and ginger, and Korean pear. As Korean Bapsang's Hyosun Ro notes: "Unlike beef bulgogi, which is marinated in soy sauce-based sauce, pork is more commonly marinated in a spicy gochujang-based sauce." This pairing of beef with soy and pork with gochujang is a fixed convention in Korean culinary tradition. The closely related jeyuk bokkeum (제육볶음) is essentially the same preparation but specifically pan-fried rather than grilled.

### Regional Japanese variations

**Osaka's Tsuruhashi district** — Japan's largest Koreatown with 800+ shops — is where yakiniku fusion runs deepest. Family-run yakiniku restaurants, many operated by Zainichi Koreans descended from Jeju Island immigrants for 50+ years, guard proprietary "nama-dare" (raw sauce) recipes passed down through generations. Bold, assertive flavors and miso-heavy marinades define the Osaka style. Shokudoen (est. 1946) was the first restaurant to devise unique sauces for each cut of meat and the first in the world to introduce smokeless roasters.

**Tokyo** trends more refined and wagyu-focused, with premium yakiniku evolving into omakase-style counter experiences at ¥20,000–36,000+ per person. The salt (shio) style is more prevalent at high-end establishments. **Tohoku** prefers tangy miso marinades reflecting the region's strong fermentation traditions. **Hokkaido** has its own entirely separate BBQ tradition — jingisukan (ジンギスカン), using lamb on dome-shaped cast-iron pans, inspired by Chinese grilled mutton.

### The Gyu-Kaku model and modern fusion

Gyu-Kaku (founded in Yokohama, first U.S. location in West Los Angeles, 2001, now 800+ locations worldwide) represents democratized, standardized yakiniku — consistent, accessible, bridging Korean BBQ's sociality with Japanese saucing philosophy. Their marinade lineup spans white miso (bestseller), sweet soy tare, and spicy gochujang options. The "spicy pork" flavor territory at Gyu-Kaku is essentially a sweetened gochujang tare applied to thin-sliced belly — the exact fusion zone we're targeting.

The most significant contemporary yakiniku evolution is **"Meat Kappo"** — treating yakiniku like kaiseki, with chef-grilled 8–12 course meals at counter seating. These chefs adjust both momi-dare and tsuke-dare for each specific cut based on fat content, fiber structure, and texture. This represents the cutting edge of yakiniku technique.

### Key takeaways

The target "sweet-spicy-savory yakiniku" flavor sits at the intersection of Japanese yakiniku tare (sweet-savory) and Korean dwaeji bulgogi (spicy-sweet-savory). Traditional yakiniku is NOT spicy — spice is a Korean contribution. The Osaka/Tsuruhashi yakiniku tradition is the most authentic home for this fusion. Gyu-Kaku's model confirms the commercial viability and popularity of this hybrid approach. For stylistic positioning, the recipe should be understood as Korean-influenced yakiniku rather than pure yakiniku or pure Korean BBQ.

## 5. Common pitfalls and the pro tips that fix them

### The three mistakes that ruin the dish

**Overcrowding the cooking surface** is the single most common home cook error across virtually every source consulted. When pork belly pieces overlap or crowd the grill/pan, they steam instead of sear, producing gray, flabby meat instead of caramelized, charred edges. The solution is ruthlessly simple: cook in small batches with space between each piece, even if it takes multiple rounds.

**Burning the sugary marinade** is the second most impactful failure mode. Gochujang contains fermented rice sugars; combined with added sweetener, the sugar load is substantial. Chef Ji Hye Kim warns explicitly: "Gochujang and doenjang burn faster than you'd think. Cook on medium to medium-high heat, NEVER the highest temperature." The fixes: shake off excess marinade before cooking, use the two-zone grilling method (sear then move to indirect), and keep sweetener quantities calibrated.

**Over-marinating with enzymatic ingredients** creates the dreaded mushy texture. America's Test Kitchen found that fruit enzyme tenderizers make meat "mushy on the surface" after 30 minutes for thin cuts. The solution: limit Asian pear or apple to ¼ cup grated per pound, keep kiwi exposure under 30 minutes, and for ¼-inch slices, cap total marination at 12 hours with mild marinades and 4 hours with enzyme-heavy ones.

### Pro tips that make the biggest difference

**Make the marinade ahead and let it rest** — garlic, ginger, and sesame oil flavors meld significantly as the sauce cools. Prepare it the night before for the best-integrated flavor.

**Reserve half the marinade before adding raw meat.** Use the clean portion for basting during grilling and for making finishing sauces (the Momofuku trick: mix with Kewpie mayo for a rich dipping sauce). This avoids contamination concerns and gives you a fresh, bright-tasting sauce alongside the cooked version.

**Add sesame oil last** when mixing the marinade, or use it exclusively as a finishing drizzle. Multiple sources found that sesame oil in the marinade interferes with flavor penetration into the meat, and its low smoke point (320–350°F for toasted) means it turns acrid on a hot grill.

**Score the meat with hidden cuts** (kakushi-bocho): professional yakiniku chefs make shallow diagonal scores across pork belly surfaces. This severs fibers for tenderness, prevents curling on the grill, and creates channels for marinade to penetrate deeper.

**Grate, don't chop, the fruit and onion.** Grating releases enzymes more effectively and creates a paste-like marinade consistency that physically adheres to the meat surface better than a thin liquid.

**Pat meat dry before it hits the grill** — even with marinade, a quick pat removes excess surface moisture. Water must evaporate before browning can begin (the temperature can't exceed 212°F while moisture remains), so removing that barrier accelerates Maillard browning.

**Grill marinated meats last** if cooking multiple items — gochujang-based marinades leave sticky, burnt residue on grill grates that will taint everything cooked afterward.

### Key takeaways

The three pillars of success: don't crowd the cooking surface, manage sugar-to-heat ratios, and respect enzymatic marination windows. The highest-leverage pro techniques are hidden scoring, pre-made marinade rest, reserved clean marinade for finishing, and patting dry before grilling. The texture spectrum from "chewy" to "mushy" is controlled almost entirely by slice thickness, enzymatic exposure time, and heat management.

## Synthesis: where the research converges and diverges

### Where sources most strongly agree

Near-universal consensus exists on these points: soy sauce, garlic, sesame oil, a sweetener, and ginger form the non-negotiable base for any yakiniku or Korean BBQ pork belly marinade. For the spicy version, gochujang is the essential heat-and-flavor driver. Thin slicing (⅛–¼ inch) is mandatory for the yakiniku approach — it transforms marinades from surface treatments into full-penetration seasoning. High, dry heat with brief cook times (2–3 minutes total for ¼-inch slices) is universally endorsed. Every respected source emphasizes fresh garlic and ginger over powdered.

### Biggest unresolved debates

**Pre-marinade vs. post-grill tare.** Traditional yakiniku purists insist on grilling plain and dipping afterward; Korean tradition and the spicy flavor target demand pre-marination. The practical resolution: for the sweet-spicy-savory target, pre-marinade is the right approach, but having a finishing tare or dipping sauce (possibly miso-based or Kewpie-blended) adds depth.

**Sugar in the marinade vs. as a finishing glaze.** AmazingRibs and food science authorities recommend saving sugar for the end to avoid burning. Korean and Japanese home cooks include it throughout. The resolution: use moderate sugar in the marinade (not excessive), shake off excess before grilling, and manage heat with the two-zone method.

**⅛ inch vs. ¼ inch thickness.** Thinner is more traditional and cooks faster; thicker gives more forgiveness with sugary marinades and better fat rendering. For the home-grill, marinated preparation: **¼ inch wins on practicality**.

**Marination duration.** Ranges from "30 minutes minimum" to "overnight." The science says that for ¼-inch slices, penetration maxes out fairly quickly. The practical answer: **4–8 hours in the refrigerator** balances flavor development with texture preservation.

### Most authoritative sources and why

**For the Korean spicy pork tradition**: Korean Bapsang (Hyosun Ro) — highest-rated with largest sample size, Korean-born authority, scientifically informed approach. Maangchi — the most influential Korean cooking voice in English, canonical recipe with decades of community testing.

**For Japanese yakiniku technique**: Just One Cookbook (Namiko Hirasawa Chen) — Japanese-born, most authoritative English-language yakiniku resource, with tested practical adaptations for home grilling.

**For professional calibration**: The Mitchell Davis / Junghyun Park cookbook recipe — James Beard Foundation pedigree, most precise ratios, represents trained Korean chef standards.

**For food science**: Serious Eats / J. Kenji López-Alt and AmazingRibs / Meathead — the gold standards for science-backed cooking technique, particularly on marinade penetration, Maillard reaction management, and enzymatic tenderization.

### Recommended priority ingredients for recipe synthesis

**Tier 1 — Essential**: Soy sauce (naturally brewed), gochujang (Sempio or CJ Haechandle), garlic (fresh, generous), ginger (fresh grated), mirin (hon-mirin), dark brown sugar, toasted sesame oil (Kadoya, used as finisher).

**Tier 2 — Highly recommended**: Gochugaru flakes (dual-chili layering), grated Asian pear or Fuji apple (enzymatic tenderizer + sweetness), grated onion (tenderizer), sake (odor removal + flavor carrier).

**Tier 3 — Pro-level upgrades**: White miso (1 Tbsp, bridges yakiniku and Korean traditions), fish sauce (1–2 tsp, background umami), MSG (¼ tsp, umami amplifier), katakuriko/potato starch (marinade cling).

### Recommended priority techniques

Start with skin-off pork belly, sliced ¼ inch thick against the grain (pre-sliced from Asian grocer or par-freeze and cut at home). Score surfaces lightly with diagonal hidden cuts. Build the marinade the night before and let it rest. Grate (don't chop) the fruit and onion. Marinate 4–8 hours in the refrigerator. Pat off excess marinade before grilling. Use a two-zone charcoal setup at 450°F — sear 1 minute per side over direct heat, then finish briefly on indirect. Flip once using the juice-beading signal. Eat immediately off the grill. Garnish with toasted sesame seeds and sliced scallions. Serve with steamed rice, lettuce wraps, ssamjang, and kimchi. Reserve clean marinade for a Kewpie mayo dipping sauce.
